# Chemotherapy: Principles and Major Drug Classes

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- **Explain** the foundational premise of cytotoxic chemotherapy — and **state precisely where that premise is wrong** — using the relationship between cell division rate and drug vulnerability.
- **Classify** the major chemotherapy drug classes (alkylating agents, antimetabolites, antitumor antibiotics, microtubule inhibitors, topoisomerase inhibitors) by mechanism, and **predict** the normal tissue each class will damage.
- **Distinguish** cell-cycle-specific from cell-cycle-nonspecific drugs and **explain** why this distinction matters for scheduling.
- **Justify** the logic of combination chemotherapy — different mechanisms, non-overlapping toxicities, single-agent activity — using a named curative regimen such as MOPP or CHOP.
- **Apply** the concept of the therapeutic window and dose intensity to **produce** a reasoned dosing decision.

## Opening Case

A first-year oncology trainee is asked why chemotherapy causes hair loss, mouth sores, and low blood counts. She gives the textbook answer: "Chemotherapy kills rapidly dividing cells. Cancer cells divide fast, so they're hit hardest; hair, gut lining, and bone marrow also divide fast, so they're collateral damage." Her attending nods, then asks a follow-up that stops her cold: "Then why does high-dose chemotherapy wipe out a patient's hematopoietic stem cells — which divide *faster* than many of the slow-growing tumor cells we're trying to kill? If 'divides faster' were the whole story, the marrow would survive and the tumor would die. It's often the reverse."

The trainee has inherited a premise that is *mostly* true and *partly* false, and she has mistaken the slogan for the mechanism. The slogan — fast division equals vulnerability — explains the toxicities well enough that it feels complete. But it cannot explain why chemotherapy works in slow-growing tumors, why it spares some fast-dividing normal tissues, or why combination regimens are built the way they are. The gap between the slogan and the real mechanism is exactly what she needs to close before she can dose a drug, build a regimen, or explain a side effect she didn't expect. This chapter closes it.

## Core Concepts

Cancer chemotherapy began with a wartime accident. In 1943, an explosion in Bari harbor released **nitrogen mustard** — a chemical weapon — into the water, and soldiers exposed to it developed profound suppression of bone marrow and lymphoid tissue (cba-37). The inference was immediate: a chemical that depletes proliferating cells might deplete a proliferating tumor. By 1946 nitrogen mustard had been used to treat lymphoma at Yale, and the era of cytotoxic chemotherapy began (cba-37).

### The premise, and where it breaks

**Cytotoxic chemotherapy** — "cytotoxic" meaning cell-killing — is the use of drugs that damage cellular machinery (DNA, nucleotide supply, the mitotic spindle) to kill cancer cells. The founding premise was that drugs interfering with DNA synthesis or mitosis preferentially kill rapidly dividing cells (cba-37).

Here is the correction the opening case demands. Many cancer cells **do not** divide faster than normal cells; hematopoietic stem cells in the marrow divide more rapidly than many tumor cells, and the proliferation differential between cancer and normal tissue is often modest (cba-37). Chemotherapy works as well as it does not merely because tumor cells divide fast, but because cancer cells carry **specific defects** — in DNA repair, in **apoptosis** (programmed cell death) regulation, in metabolic flexibility — that make them less able to survive the damage the drug inflicts (cba-37). A normal cell takes a DNA hit, pauses, repairs it, and continues. A tumor cell with broken repair machinery takes the same hit and dies. The slogan tracks the *toxicities* (which fall on genuinely fast-dividing normal tissue); the *anti-tumor effect* tracks vulnerability, not just speed.

### The therapeutic window

The **therapeutic window** is the gap between the dose that kills enough cancer and the dose that harms the patient unacceptably. For cytotoxic chemotherapy this window is narrow, because the same machinery the drug attacks in the tumor exists in normal proliferating tissue (cba-37). Every dosing decision, every supportive-care intervention, and every combination strategy in this chapter is an attempt to widen or exploit that window.

### Cell-cycle specificity

The **cell cycle** is the ordered sequence a dividing cell passes through: G1 (growth), S (DNA synthesis), G2 (preparation for division), and M (mitosis), with non-dividing cells resting in **G0**. A drug is **cell-cycle-specific** if it acts only during a particular phase — antimetabolites, for example, damage cells chiefly in **S phase**, when DNA is being copied (cba-37). A drug is **cell-cycle-nonspecific** if it can damage cells in any phase, including resting cells — alkylating agents largely fall here. The distinction has a practical consequence: cell-cycle-specific drugs benefit from prolonged exposure or repeated dosing (to catch cells as they cycle into the vulnerable phase), while nonspecific drugs act more on a dose-proportional basis.

### The major drug classes

**Alkylating agents** bind covalently to DNA, transferring alkyl groups to nucleophilic sites — most commonly the **N7 position of guanine** — producing monoadducts, intrastrand crosslinks, and the most lethal lesion, **interstrand crosslinks**, which physically prevent the two DNA strands from separating for replication (cba-37). **Cyclophosphamide** is the prototype; it is a prodrug activated by liver cytochrome P450 to phosphoramide mustard, with a toxic byproduct, acrolein, that causes hemorrhagic cystitis unless neutralized by **mesna** (cba-37). The **platinum compounds** — **cisplatin, carboplatin, oxaliplatin** — form DNA crosslinks by a functionally similar mechanism; cisplatin cures most metastatic testicular cancer and is one of the most important cancer drugs ever made (cba-37).

**Antimetabolites** resemble normal metabolites (purines, pyrimidines, folates) and jam nucleotide biosynthesis or get mis-incorporated into DNA; they are S-phase-specific (cba-37). **Methotrexate** inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, starving the cell of the tetrahydrofolate needed to make DNA bases; at high doses it is given with **leucovorin rescue** to protect normal cells (cba-37). **5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)** inhibits thymidylate synthase and anchors colorectal regimens (FOLFOX, FOLFIRI) (cba-37).

**Antitumor antibiotics** — natural products mostly from *Streptomyces* — damage DNA by several routes at once. **Doxorubicin** (an anthracycline) intercalates into DNA, generates free radicals, and inhibits topoisomerase II; its signature limit is **cumulative cardiotoxicity**, capped near 450–550 mg/m² lifetime dose (cba-37). **Bleomycin** causes **pulmonary fibrosis** and likewise carries a cumulative dose limit (cba-37).

**Microtubule (mitotic) inhibitors** attack the spindle. **Vinca alkaloids** (vincristine, vinblastine) *prevent* microtubule polymerization; **taxanes** (paclitaxel, docetaxel) do the opposite — they *stabilize* microtubules so rigidly the spindle cannot function (cba-37). Both arrest cells in mitosis; both cause cumulative **peripheral neuropathy** as their dose-limiting toxicity (cba-37). Crucially, vincristine causes minimal myelosuppression, which is why it is so useful in heavily pre-treated patients (cba-37).

**Topoisomerase inhibitors** trap the enzymes that manage DNA tangling with the DNA broken. **Irinotecan** and **topotecan** target topoisomerase I; **etoposide** targets topoisomerase II and carries a real risk of **secondary leukemia** (typically AML with 11q23 translocations) in 1–2% of recipients (cba-37).

### Combination logic

No single drug cures most cancers, because a single mutation can confer resistance. **Combination chemotherapy**, articulated by Frei, DeVita and others in the 1960s, follows three rules: combine drugs with **different mechanisms** (so one mutation cannot defeat all), with **non-overlapping toxicities** (so each can be given near full dose without lethal cumulative harm), and each with **demonstrated single-agent activity** (cba-37). The **MOPP** regimen (mechlorethamine, vincristine, procarbazine, prednisone) cured what had been uniformly fatal Hodgkin lymphoma; **R-CHOP** (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone) is standard for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma; **BEP** (bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin) cures most metastatic testicular cancer (cba-37).

## Worked Example

**Situation.** A patient needs **carboplatin**, a platinum alkylating-type agent eliminated almost entirely by the kidneys. Carboplatin is unusual: unlike most cytotoxics, it is not dosed by body surface area but by a target **AUC** (area under the concentration–time curve), because its effect and its marrow toxicity track total drug exposure, and that exposure depends directly on kidney function. We want a target AUC of 5 mg·min/mL. The patient's glomerular filtration rate (GFR — the kidney's filtration rate) is estimated at 90 mL/min.

**Reasoning — the dead end.** The instinct is to dose like everything else: look up a milligrams-per-square-meter number and multiply by body surface area. But carboplatin's clearance is essentially renal, and two patients of identical size can have very different kidney function. Dose by surface area alone and you over-dose the patient with poor kidneys (severe thrombocytopenia) and under-dose the patient with excellent kidneys (lost efficacy). Surface area is the wrong variable; **renal clearance** is the right one. This is exactly the therapeutic-window problem made quantitative.

**Reasoning — the Calvert formula.** The correct tool is the **Calvert formula**:

> Dose (mg) = target AUC × (GFR + 25)

The "+25" approximates non-renal clearance. Plugging in:

> Dose = 5 × (90 + 25) = 5 × 115 = **575 mg**

So the patient receives 575 mg of carboplatin — derived from the target exposure and their kidney function, not their height and weight.

**Reasoning — anticipating the nadir.** Carboplatin's dose-limiting toxicity is **thrombocytopenia** (low platelets). The **nadir** — the lowest blood-count point after a dose — typically falls about 7–14 days after most chemotherapy (cba-38, applied here). So we counsel the patient to expect platelets to bottom out in the second week, schedule a blood count then, and time the next cycle only after recovery. We have turned a vague "watch for side effects" into a dated calendar entry.

**The lesson.** Dosing is mechanism plus pharmacology, not a lookup table. Carboplatin is dosed to a target *exposure* because its benefit and its harm both scale with exposure, and exposure is governed by renal clearance — so we measure the kidney and compute backward with the Calvert formula.

**The limit.** This works precisely because carboplatin is renally cleared and has a predictable, marrow-limited toxicity. It does not generalize to drugs cleared by the liver, dosed by surface area, or limited by a *cumulative* toxicity such as doxorubicin's cardiomyopathy — where the relevant ceiling is lifetime total dose, not single-cycle exposure (cba-37). The skill is matching the dosing logic to the drug's actual elimination and toxicity.

## Common Misconceptions

**"Chemotherapy works because cancer cells divide faster than normal cells."** This is the opening case's slogan, and it is the most consequential half-truth in oncology. It fails because many tumors divide *slower* than hematopoietic stem cells, yet chemotherapy still kills the tumor and the marrow recovers; the anti-tumor effect depends on tumor-specific defects in DNA repair and apoptosis, not on raw division speed (cba-37). Believing the slogan leaves you unable to explain why slow-growing lymphomas respond, or why the marrow isn't permanently destroyed.

**"You can keep escalating the dose to kill more cancer — more drug, more kill."** It fails on the therapeutic window. The same machinery the drug attacks in the tumor exists in normal proliferating tissue, so past a point, added dose buys cumulative or lethal toxicity (febrile neutropenia, cardiomyopathy, neuropathy) faster than added tumor kill (cba-37). Dose intensity matters, but only within tolerance — which is why combination regimens exploit *non-overlapping* toxicities to deliver near-full doses of several drugs rather than a crushing dose of one.

**"A drug class is just a naming convention — knowing the class doesn't tell you much."** It fails because the class *is* the mechanism, and the mechanism predicts the toxicity. Knowing a drug is an anthracycline tells you to track cumulative cardiac dose; knowing it is a vinca alkaloid or taxane tells you to watch for neuropathy; knowing it is a topoisomerase II inhibitor tells you to counsel about secondary leukemia risk (cba-37). The class is a prediction engine, not a filing label.

## What Would Change My Mind

The central claim here is that chemotherapy's anti-tumor effect is driven primarily by tumor-specific vulnerabilities (defective DNA repair, dysregulated apoptosis), not merely by faster proliferation. A finding that would seriously revise this: a rigorous study across many tumor types showing that *proliferation rate alone* — measured directly, e.g., by Ki-67 index — predicts chemotherapy response far better than markers of DNA-repair deficiency or apoptotic competence, with the repair/apoptosis markers adding little once proliferation is accounted for. If, conversely, restoring proficient DNA repair in a tumor (without changing its growth rate) reliably abolished chemosensitivity, that would *strengthen* the claim. The cleanest counterexample would be a slow-growing, repair-proficient, apoptosis-competent tumor that is nonetheless reliably cured by cytotoxic chemotherapy — which the vulnerability model predicts should not happen. ## Still Puzzling

- The premise that cytotoxic drugs exploit tumor-specific vulnerabilities is now widely accepted, yet we still cannot, for most patients, *measure those vulnerabilities up front* and pick the drug accordingly. Why has predictive biomarker-driven chemotherapy selection lagged so far behind the mechanistic understanding?
- Cross-resistance among microtubule inhibitors is **incomplete** — taxane-resistant tumors sometimes respond to vinca alkaloids or eribulin, and vice versa (cba-37). What determines which resistance pathways a given tumor takes, and could that be predicted rather than discovered by trial and error?
- New chemotherapy regimen development has **slowed since the 2000s** (cba-37), with innovation shifting to targeted and immune therapies. Are the classical combinations genuinely near-optimal, or have we simply stopped looking for better cytotoxic combinations because attention and funding moved elsewhere?
