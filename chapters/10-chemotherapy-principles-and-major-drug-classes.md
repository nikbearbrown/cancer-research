# Chapter 10 — Chemotherapy: Principles and Major Drug Classes

A first-year oncology trainee is asked why chemotherapy causes hair loss, mouth sores, and low blood counts. She gives the textbook answer: chemotherapy kills rapidly dividing cells. Cancer cells divide fast, so they are hit hardest; hair follicles, gut lining, and bone marrow also divide fast, so they are collateral damage. Her attending nods, then asks a follow-up that stops her cold: "Then why does high-dose chemotherapy wipe out a patient's hematopoietic stem cells — which divide faster than many of the slow-growing tumor cells we are trying to kill? If 'divides faster' were the whole story, the marrow would survive and the tumor would die. It is often the reverse."

The trainee has inherited a premise that is mostly true and partly false, and she has mistaken the slogan for the mechanism. The slogan — fast division equals vulnerability — explains the toxicities well enough that it feels complete. It cannot explain why chemotherapy works in slow-growing tumors, why it spares some fast-dividing normal tissues, or why combination regimens are built the way they are. Closing that gap is what this chapter is for.

---

## How chemotherapy began

Cancer chemotherapy began with a wartime accident. In 1943, an explosion in Bari harbor released nitrogen mustard — a chemical weapon — into the water. Soldiers exposed to it developed profound suppression of bone marrow and lymphoid tissue. The inference was immediate: a chemical that depletes proliferating cells might deplete a proliferating tumor. By 1946, nitrogen mustard had been used to treat lymphoma at Yale, and the era of cytotoxic chemotherapy began.

**Cytotoxic** means cell-killing. The founding premise was that drugs interfering with DNA synthesis or mitosis preferentially kill rapidly dividing cells, because those cells are actively using the machinery being attacked. The premise was productive — it generated decades of drug development and genuinely curative regimens. It is also incomplete in a specific and important way.

---

## The premise, and where it breaks

Many cancer cells do not divide faster than normal cells. Hematopoietic stem cells in the marrow divide more rapidly than many tumor cells, and the proliferation rate of cancer cells, when measured directly, often falls in the same range as normal proliferating tissue.

Chemotherapy works as well as it does not merely because tumor cells divide fast, but because cancer cells carry specific defects — in DNA repair, in apoptosis regulation, in metabolic flexibility — that make them less able to survive the damage the drug inflicts. A normal hematopoietic stem cell takes a DNA hit from cyclophosphamide, pauses, activates its checkpoint, repairs the double-strand break through homologous recombination, and continues. A tumor cell with defective mismatch repair, or mutated p53, or suppressed apoptotic machinery takes the same hit — and dies, or accumulates lethal mutations that will kill it.

The slogan tracks the *toxicities*: hair follicles, gut lining, and bone marrow divide fast, and the drugs do fall disproportionately on them. That part is accurate. The *anti-tumor effect*, however, tracks vulnerability, not just speed. The distinction matters practically: it explains why slow-growing lymphomas respond to chemotherapy, why some fast-dividing tissues recover when others do not, and why acquiring a drug-resistance mutation — typically one that restores a repair pathway or apoptotic competence — is the most common route to treatment failure.

---

## The therapeutic window

The **therapeutic window** is the gap between the dose that kills enough cancer and the dose that harms the patient unacceptably. For cytotoxic chemotherapy this window is narrow, because the machinery the drug attacks in the tumor exists in normal proliferating tissue too. Every dosing decision, every supportive-care intervention, and every combination strategy in this chapter is an attempt to exploit or widen that window.

Dose too little and you achieve insufficient tumor kill, with resistance emerging from surviving cells. Dose too much and the toxicities — marrow suppression, gut damage, organ toxicity — become life-threatening faster than the tumor kill extends life. The therapeutic window is the space in between, and it is what determines how chemotherapy is scheduled, combined, and monitored.

---

## Cell-cycle specificity

The cell cycle runs: **G1** (growth, biosynthesis), **S** (DNA synthesis), **G2** (preparation for division), **M** (mitosis). Cells not actively cycling sit in **G0** — quiescent, resting. A drug that acts only in a particular phase is **cell-cycle-specific**; a drug that can damage cells regardless of phase is **cell-cycle-nonspecific**.

Antimetabolites, which jam the DNA synthesis machinery, are S-phase-specific: they work only when the cell is actively copying its DNA. A cancer cell in G0 when the drug is present is unharmed; the same cell cycling into S phase two days later is vulnerable. The practical consequence is that S-phase-specific drugs benefit from prolonged exposure or divided dosing — to catch cells as they enter the vulnerable phase — rather than a single high peak.

Alkylating agents, by contrast, can damage DNA in any phase, including resting cells. Their effects are more dose-proportional, and they can damage G0 cells that an antimetabolite would miss. This is one reason alkylating agents, at high enough doses, can ablate hematopoietic stem cells that might otherwise survive a cycle-specific drug.

## The five major drug classes

**Alkylating agents** bind covalently to DNA, transferring alkyl groups to nucleophilic sites — most commonly the N7 position of guanine. They produce monoadducts, intrastrand crosslinks, and the most lethal lesion: interstrand crosslinks, which physically prevent the two DNA strands from separating for replication. **Cyclophosphamide** is the prototype. It is a prodrug activated by liver cytochrome P450 to phosphoramide mustard; a toxic byproduct, acrolein, accumulates in the bladder and causes hemorrhagic cystitis unless neutralized by **mesna**. The **platinum compounds** — cisplatin, carboplatin, oxaliplatin — form DNA adducts and crosslinks by a functionally similar mechanism. Cisplatin cures the large majority of metastatic testicular cancers. Carboplatin is dosed differently from most chemotherapy (more on this below). Oxaliplatin is the backbone of colorectal cancer regimens and causes a distinctive cold-triggered peripheral neuropathy in addition to its standard cumulative neuropathy.

**Antimetabolites** resemble normal metabolites — purines, pyrimidines, folates — and either jam nucleotide biosynthesis or get incorporated into DNA as fraudulent building blocks. They are S-phase-specific. **Methotrexate** inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, starving the cell of the tetrahydrofolate needed to synthesize DNA bases. At high doses, **leucovorin rescue** — supplying the downstream folate product directly — is given to protect normal cells while tumor cells, which may have impaired uptake of leucovorin, are less protected. **5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)** inhibits thymidylate synthase and is the backbone of most colorectal regimens. **Gemcitabine** is incorporated into DNA and stalls the replication machinery, and is central to pancreatic cancer treatment.

**Antitumor antibiotics** are natural products, mostly from *Streptomyces*, that damage DNA by several mechanisms simultaneously. **Doxorubicin** (an anthracycline) intercalates between DNA base pairs, generates free radicals, and inhibits topoisomerase II. Its signature dose-limiting toxicity is cumulative **cardiomyopathy**, capped near a lifetime dose of 450 to 550 mg/m² — beyond which the risk of heart failure rises sharply. **Bleomycin** causes direct double-strand DNA breaks but accumulates in the lung and causes **pulmonary fibrosis**, which also carries a cumulative dose limit. Both drugs require lifetime tracking — not just cycle-by-cycle monitoring, but a running total.

**Microtubule inhibitors** attack the mitotic spindle. **Vinca alkaloids** — vincristine, vinblastine — prevent microtubule polymerization, so the spindle cannot form. **Taxanes** — paclitaxel, docetaxel — do the opposite: they stabilize microtubules so rigidly that the spindle cannot function. Both classes arrest cells in mitosis; both cause cumulative **peripheral neuropathy** as the dose-limiting toxicity. Vincristine has the important property of causing minimal myelosuppression, making it useful in heavily pretreated patients whose bone marrow reserve is depleted.

**Topoisomerase inhibitors** trap the enzymes that manage DNA coiling by holding them covalently bound to broken DNA. **Irinotecan** and **topotecan** target topoisomerase I; **etoposide** targets topoisomerase II. Etoposide carries a specific risk of **secondary leukemia** — typically AML with 11q23 translocations — in 1 to 2% of recipients, arising from etoposide-induced translocations in hematopoietic progenitors. This risk is real, is discussed with patients before treatment, and appears in the survivorship follow-up of patients treated with etoposide-containing regimens.

## Combination chemotherapy

No single drug cures most cancers, because a single mutation can confer resistance to that drug's mechanism, and such mutations arise readily in tumors with high proliferation rates and genomic instability. Combination chemotherapy, developed in the 1960s, follows three classical rules: combine drugs with **different mechanisms** (so a single resistance mutation cannot defeat all of them), with **non-overlapping toxicities** (so each can be given at near-full dose without lethal cumulative harm), and each with **demonstrated single-agent activity** (so each is contributing to tumor kill, not just adding toxicity).

**MOPP** — mechlorethamine, vincristine (Oncovin), procarbazine, prednisone — was the first regimen to cure what had been uniformly fatal Hodgkin lymphoma. Mechlorethamine is an alkylating agent; vincristine is a microtubule inhibitor with no marrow toxicity; procarbazine is a methylating agent; prednisone is a corticosteroid. Different mechanisms, largely non-overlapping dose-limiting toxicities, and each had demonstrated single-agent activity in lymphoma.

**R-CHOP** — rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone — is the standard regimen for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma. Rituximab is a monoclonal antibody targeting CD20 on B cells; cyclophosphamide is an alkylating agent; doxorubicin is an anthracycline; vincristine is the non-myelosuppressive microtubule inhibitor; prednisone is a steroid. The combination of an immune agent, an alkylator, a topoisomerase/intercalator, and a spindle poison covers multiple mechanisms.

**BEP** — bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin — cures most metastatic testicular cancer. Cisplatin provides the interstrand crosslinks; etoposide targets topoisomerase II; bleomycin provides direct DNA breaks. The curative potential of BEP in a disseminated solid tumor was among the most striking demonstrations of combination chemotherapy's power.

## Dosing carboplatin: the therapeutic window made quantitative

Most chemotherapy is dosed by body surface area — a rough proxy for the volume of distribution. Carboplatin is an exception worth understanding in detail, because it makes the therapeutic window argument concrete.

Carboplatin is eliminated almost entirely by the kidneys. Its toxicity and its efficacy both scale with total drug exposure — the area under the concentration-time curve, the AUC. Two patients of identical size can have very different kidney function, and therefore very different exposure from the same surface-area-based dose. Give too little and you lose efficacy; give too much and you produce severe thrombocytopenia, the dose-limiting toxicity.

The **Calvert formula** solves this:

> Dose (mg) = target AUC × (GFR + 25)

GFR is the glomerular filtration rate, measured or estimated from creatinine clearance. The 25 term approximates non-renal clearance. For a target AUC of 5 in a patient with a GFR of 90 mL/min:

> Dose = 5 × (90 + 25) = 575 mg

The patient receives 575 mg — derived from the target exposure and their kidney function, not from their height and weight. In a patient with a GFR of 40 (significantly impaired kidneys), the same target AUC would give:

> Dose = 5 × (40 + 25) = 325 mg

The same surface area might suggest a larger dose, but the patient with impaired kidneys clears carboplatin slowly, accumulates more drug, and would be severely overtreated without the correction.

Carboplatin's dose-limiting toxicity, thrombocytopenia, reaches its **nadir** — the lowest platelet count after a dose — at approximately 7 to 14 days after treatment. Planning around the nadir means scheduling blood counts in the second week, timing the next cycle only after platelet recovery, and counseling the patient specifically that the second week carries the highest bleeding risk.

This is dosing as mechanism and pharmacology, not as a lookup table. The lesson generalizes: doxorubicin requires lifetime dose tracking because its toxicity is cumulative; bleomycin requires pulmonary function monitoring for the same reason; etoposide requires long-term leukemia surveillance. Each drug's properties determine its monitoring, and knowing the class is knowing what to watch for.

## What would change this picture

The central claim is that chemotherapy's anti-tumor effect is driven primarily by tumor-specific vulnerabilities — in DNA repair, apoptosis, and metabolic flexibility — not merely by faster proliferation. The finding that would revise this: rigorous studies across many tumor types showing that proliferation rate alone, measured directly by Ki-67 or another index, predicts chemotherapy response far better than markers of DNA-repair deficiency or apoptotic competence, with the repair and apoptosis markers adding negligible predictive value once proliferation is accounted for. The cleanest counterexample would be a slow-growing, repair-proficient, apoptosis-competent tumor reliably cured by cytotoxic chemotherapy — which the vulnerability model predicts should not happen.

---

## Still open

Why predictive biomarker-driven chemotherapy selection has lagged so far behind the mechanistic understanding is genuinely puzzling. We know that cisplatin sensitivity tracks BRCA2 status in ovarian cancer; we know that MMR status predicts response to some agents. But for most drugs and most cancers, we still cannot measure the vulnerabilities that the drugs are supposed to exploit and choose accordingly before treatment begins.

Cross-resistance among microtubule inhibitors is incomplete. Taxane-resistant tumors sometimes respond to vinca alkaloids, and vice versa, which means the resistance mechanisms are not identical. Whether the specific resistance pathway a tumor takes is predictable — rather than discovered by sequential treatment failures — is an open question with significant clinical implications.

New combination chemotherapy regimen development has slowed markedly since the early 2000s, with innovation shifting to targeted and immune therapies. Whether the classical combinations are genuinely near-optimal or whether the search for better cytotoxic combinations has simply been displaced by funding and attention is not settled.
