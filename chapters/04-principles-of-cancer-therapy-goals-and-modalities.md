# Principles of Cancer Therapy: Goals and Modalities

## Learning Objectives

After working through this chapter, you should be able to:

- **Identify** the goal of a cancer treatment — cure, control, palliation, or comfort — and explain why the goal, not the cancer type alone, determines the approach.
- **Distinguish** neoadjuvant, adjuvant, concurrent, consolidation, and maintenance therapy by *when* they are given relative to definitive local treatment and *why* the timing matters.
- **Differentiate** surrogate endpoints (tumor shrinkage, response rate, progression-free survival) from hard endpoints (overall survival, quality of life), and explain when a surrogate can mislead.
- **Evaluate** a treatment claim by asking which endpoint it rests on and whether that endpoint has been validated against survival.
- **Construct** a multimodality treatment plan and explain how surgery, radiation, and systemic therapy combine.

## Opening Case

A new targeted drug for a metastatic solid tumor is approved on the strength of a striking statistic: in its pivotal trial, it produced an **overall response rate of 60%** — six in ten patients had their tumors shrink by at least 30% on imaging — compared with 20% for the standard regimen. The press release calls it a breakthrough. A patient's family, reading that tumors shrank three times as often, wants this drug.

The oncologist looks past the headline to the rest of the trial. The drug improved the response rate and lengthened the time until tumors started growing again (progression-free survival). But the trial's **overall survival** curves — how long patients actually lived — were nearly identical between the two arms. Tumors shrank more, and shrank for longer, but patients did not live longer. Meanwhile, the new drug caused more severe side effects, so the patients on it spent more of their remaining time managing toxicity.

So did the drug work? It depends entirely on what "work" means. It shrank tumors — a real, measurable effect. But tumor shrinkage was being treated as a stand-in for the thing everyone actually cares about: living longer and better. When the stand-in moved and the real outcome did not, the breakthrough dissolved. This chapter is about that gap — between what a treatment *does to the tumor* and what it *does for the patient* — and about the framework of treatment goals and endpoints that keeps the two from being confused.

## Core Concepts

### The first question is the goal, not the drug

The same patient with the same cancer at the same stage can be treated in completely different ways depending on the **goal of treatment** [source chapter; NCI, *Types of Cancer Treatment*]. There are four:

- **Cure.** Eliminate the cancer so the patient lives out a normal lifespan cancer-free. Realistic for localized solid tumors and some hematologic cancers. Justifies intense, toxic, multimodality treatment.
- **Control.** Slow or halt progression when cure is unattainable but the disease can be managed for years. The goal for most metastatic cancer — the patient lives *with* cancer.
- **Palliation.** Relieve symptoms (pain, obstruction, bleeding) and improve quality of life without necessarily extending it much.
- **Comfort (hospice/end-of-life).** When disease-modifying treatment no longer helps, the focus shifts entirely to comfort.

The goal usually shifts over a disease course — cure, then control, then palliation, then comfort. Choosing treatment without first fixing the goal risks being too aggressive (harm exceeds benefit) or too passive (missed opportunity). This is the chapter's organizing principle: **treatment intent governs everything downstream**, including which endpoint counts as success.

### The three modalities

Cancer treatment uses three modalities, usually in combination [source chapter; NCI, *Types of Cancer Treatment*]:

- **Surgery.** The oldest and still the most effective treatment for many localized solid tumors. Its principles: clear margins (no tumor at the resection edge), adequate lymph-node sampling for staging, minimal disturbance of surrounding structure.
- **Radiation therapy.** Ionizing radiation kills cells through DNA damage. Used as primary treatment, after surgery (adjuvant), before surgery (neoadjuvant), for palliation, or as consolidation.
- **Systemic therapy.** Drugs that travel through the bloodstream: **chemotherapy** (cytotoxic agents disrupting DNA synthesis or mitosis), **hormonal therapy** (blocking hormones that drive growth), **targeted therapy** (small molecules and antibodies against specific molecular features [NCI, *Targeted Therapy for Cancer*]), and **immunotherapy** (activating the immune system — checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T) [NCI, *Immunotherapy to Treat Cancer*].

### Timing has a name, and the name encodes the logic

When systemic therapy or radiation is given *relative to* definitive local treatment is not incidental — it changes what the treatment is for:

- **Neoadjuvant:** given *before* the primary intervention (e.g., chemo before surgery) to shrink the tumor, enable more conservative surgery, and reveal whether the cancer responds.
- **Adjuvant:** given *after* the primary intervention to kill micrometastatic disease too small to detect, reducing recurrence.
- **Concurrent:** modalities given simultaneously (chemoradiation) for synergy.
- **Consolidation:** given after primary therapy to deepen the response.
- **Maintenance:** ongoing therapy after an initial response to prevent progression.

Adjuvant therapy is the cleanest illustration of an evidence point: you are treating disease you *cannot see*, on the statistical expectation that micrometastases exist. The justification is a population-level reduction in recurrence in trials — not visible tumor in this patient.

### Surrogate endpoints versus hard endpoints

This is the heart of the chapter. To evaluate a treatment, you measure *something*. The cleanest measures — **hard endpoints** — are the outcomes patients ultimately care about: **overall survival (OS)**, how long they live, and **quality of life**. But hard endpoints take years to mature, so trials lean on **surrogate endpoints** — faster, easier proxies expected to predict the hard outcome:

- **Overall response rate (ORR):** the fraction of patients whose tumors shrink by a defined amount (RECIST: ≥30% reduction = partial response) [source chapter].
- **Progression-free survival (PFS):** time until the tumor grows or the patient dies.
- **Biomarker response:** falling tumor-marker levels (PSA, CA-125).

A surrogate is only trustworthy if changing it reliably changes the hard endpoint. Sometimes it does (in some settings PFS tracks OS well). Sometimes it does not — a drug shrinks tumors and delays progression but patients do not live longer, as in the Opening Case. **Tumor shrinkage is a proxy for benefit, not benefit itself.** The recurring failure mode across oncology evidence is treating the proxy as the thing: response rate for survival, PFS for OS, biomarker drop for cure. The FDA's accelerated-approval pathway explicitly allows approval on surrogate endpoints, with the expectation of confirmatory trials proving real benefit — and the FDA's recent initiatives press for that confirmation and for endpoints showing *meaningful* clinical benefit [FDA, *Oncology Center of Excellence*; FDA, *Project Optimus*].

### Multidisciplinary reasoning and response assessment

Because modern treatment combines modalities, decisions are made by **multidisciplinary tumor boards** — surgeon, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, pathologist, radiologist together [source chapter]. Response during treatment is judged by **RECIST** (complete/partial response, stable disease, progressive disease) — though immunotherapy needs modified criteria (iRECIST) because **pseudoprogression**, an early apparent growth from immune-cell infiltration, can masquerade as failure before the tumor regresses [source chapter]. Even response assessment, in other words, is a measurement system with its own artifacts.

## Worked Example

**Situation.** Evaluate the Opening Case drug formally. Trial result: ORR 60% vs 20%; median PFS improved by 3 months; median OS difference not statistically significant; grade 3–4 toxicity higher in the new arm. A patient asks: "Should I take it?"

**First attempt (the dead end).** "Sixty percent response versus twenty — and longer progression-free survival. The drug is clearly better; take it." This is the proxy trap. It elevates two surrogate endpoints (ORR, PFS) to the status of benefit and never asks whether they translated into the hard endpoint. It also ignores that the drug's toxicity consumes quality of life — the *other* hard endpoint.

**Reasoning with the endpoints.** Lay the endpoints in a hierarchy:

- *ORR (surrogate):* strongly favors the new drug, but ORR measures only that tumors shrank, not that patients benefited. A high ORR with flat OS is a known, documented pattern.
- *PFS (surrogate):* favors the new drug by 3 months. PFS is a better surrogate than ORR but is *not* validated as a survival surrogate in every disease. A PFS gain with no OS gain can mean the cancer was merely held briefly before resuming, with the same eventual course.
- *OS (hard):* no significant difference. Patients did not live longer.
- *Quality of life (hard):* worse on the new drug due to higher grade 3–4 toxicity.

When the surrogates move but both hard endpoints fail to improve (OS flat, QoL worse), the honest read is that the drug demonstrably affects the *tumor* but has not demonstrated benefit for the *patient* — and has demonstrated harm to quality of life.

**Resolution.** The recommendation depends on the goal. If the patient's goal is to maximize survival, the trial gives no evidence this drug delivers it. If the goal includes quality of life, the toxicity is a strike against it. The drug is not "clearly better"; it is *better on proxies and not on outcomes*. A reasonable patient, properly informed, might decline it — or might accept it if delaying radiographic progression has personal value to them — but the decision must be made knowing the survival curves overlapped.

**The lesson.** Always ask which endpoint a treatment claim rests on, and whether that endpoint is a surrogate or a hard outcome. A change in a surrogate is a hypothesis about benefit, not benefit. Tumor shrinkage, response rate, and progression-free survival can each improve while patients live no longer and feel worse.

**The limit.** Surrogates are not worthless — they let trials read out faster, enable accelerated approval for desperate diseases, and *sometimes* do predict survival. The skill is not "ignore surrogates" but knowing, for a given cancer and endpoint, whether the surrogate has been *validated* against survival. That validation status varies by disease and is often unsettled [contested — see pantry flag], which is exactly why hard endpoints remain the arbiter when they are available.

## Common Misconceptions

**"The drug shrinks tumors more, so it works better."** Tumor shrinkage (response rate) is a surrogate endpoint. As the Opening Case and Worked Example show, a drug can triple the response rate and improve progression-free survival while overall survival stays flat and quality of life falls. Shrinkage is what the drug does to the tumor; benefit is what it does for the patient, and the two can diverge.

**"If treatment is given, there must be visible disease to treat."** Adjuvant therapy treats micrometastatic disease that is, by definition, undetectable. Its justification is a population-level reduction in recurrence demonstrated in trials, not visible tumor in the individual. Demanding visible disease before adjuvant treatment would discard one of oncology's most effective strategies.

**"Progression on immunotherapy means the drug failed."** Immune checkpoint inhibitors can cause pseudoprogression — apparent early growth from immune infiltration before regression. Standard RECIST can mislead here, which is why iRECIST requires confirming progression at a later time point before declaring failure.

**"More treatment is always better for cancer."** Treatment intent governs intensity. Aggressive multimodality therapy is justified for curative intent but can be net-harmful in palliative or comfort settings, where it adds toxicity without extending life. Matching intensity to goal — not maximizing it — is the principle.

## Exercises

1. **(Understand.)** Define neoadjuvant, adjuvant, and concurrent therapy in one sentence each, and state the distinct rationale for each in terms of *what* it is trying to accomplish and *when* it is given.

2. **(Apply.)** A trial reports that Drug X improves median progression-free survival from 6 to 9 months with no significant change in overall survival. List two scenarios in which this PFS gain is genuinely valuable to a patient and two in which it is not. State what additional data you would request before recommending the drug.

3. **(Apply+ — produce an endpoint analysis.)** Find (or construct) a hypothetical pivotal trial reporting ORR, PFS, OS, and a grade 3–4 toxicity rate. Build a small table classifying each endpoint as surrogate or hard, mark the direction and significance of each result, and write a one-paragraph recommendation that explicitly weighs the hard endpoints above the surrogates. State the treatment goal your recommendation assumes.

4. **(Produce — a decision tree.)** Draw a treatment-intent decision tree for a patient with colorectal cancer that begins localized and later becomes metastatic. At each node, mark the goal (cure/control/palliation/comfort), the dominant modality, and the endpoint by which success would be judged.

5. **(Evaluate.)** Accelerated approval lets the FDA approve drugs on surrogate endpoints, requiring confirmatory trials afterward. Argue either that this is the right trade-off (faster access for desperate patients) or the wrong one (drugs that move proxies but not survival reach the market). Ground your argument in the surrogate-versus-hard-endpoint distinction and the FDA's stated emphasis on confirmatory evidence [FDA, *Oncology Center of Excellence*].

## What Would Change My Mind

The central claim is that surrogate endpoints (response rate, PFS) can move without hard endpoints (overall survival, quality of life) following, so treatment claims must ultimately rest on hard outcomes. What would revise this? Robust, disease-specific evidence that a particular surrogate is a *validated* predictor of survival — for example, a formal trial-level meta-analysis showing that, in a given cancer, improvements in progression-free survival reliably and quantitatively translate into improvements in overall survival across many trials. Where such validation exists, the surrogate earns the right to stand in for the hard endpoint, and the strong skepticism relaxes for that setting [FDA, *Oncology Center of Excellence*; FDA, *Project Optimus*]. Conversely, continued accumulation of approved drugs that improved surrogates but never confirmed survival benefit in post-approval trials would harden the claim that proxies must be treated as hypotheses, not conclusions, until the hard endpoint is in.

## Still Puzzling

- For which cancers and which surrogates is progression-free survival actually a validated stand-in for overall survival, and for which is it not? The answer varies by disease and is often unsettled — how should a clinician act in the unsettled zone? [contested]
- When a drug improves quality of life but not survival, or survival but not quality of life, how should those two hard endpoints be weighed against each other — and by whom, the trialist or the patient?
- Early integration of palliative care has, in some trials, *improved* survival alongside quality of life (the Temel et al. 2010 lung cancer trial). If comfort-directed care can extend life, how stable is the four-goal hierarchy that treats cure and comfort as opposite ends?
