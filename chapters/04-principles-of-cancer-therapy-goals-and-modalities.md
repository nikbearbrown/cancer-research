# Chapter 4 — Principles of Cancer Therapy: Goals and Modalities

A new targeted drug for a metastatic solid tumor is approved on the strength of a striking statistic: in its pivotal trial, it produced an overall response rate of 60% — six in ten patients had their tumors shrink by at least 30% on imaging — compared with 20% for the standard regimen. The press release calls it a breakthrough. A patient's family, reading that tumors shrank three times as often, wants this drug.

The oncologist looks past the headline to the rest of the trial. The drug improved the response rate and lengthened the time until tumors started growing again. But the overall survival curves — how long patients actually lived — were nearly identical between the two arms. Tumors shrank more, and shrank for longer, but patients did not live longer. Meanwhile the new drug caused more severe side effects, so the patients on it spent more of their remaining time managing toxicity.

Did the drug work? It depends entirely on what "work" means. It shrank tumors — a real, measurable effect. But tumor shrinkage was being treated as a stand-in for the thing everyone actually cares about: living longer and better. When the stand-in moved and the real outcome did not, the breakthrough dissolved.

This chapter is about that gap — between what a treatment does to the tumor and what it does for the patient — and about the framework of treatment goals and endpoints that keeps the two from being confused.

---

## The goal comes first

The same patient with the same cancer at the same stage can be treated in completely different ways depending on the goal of treatment. There are four, and they are not a progression toward one endpoint but genuinely different objectives.

**Cure** means eliminating the cancer so the patient lives a normal lifespan cancer-free. Cure is realistic for localized solid tumors before metastasis and for some hematologic malignancies. The treatment justification changes when cure is the goal: intense, toxic, multimodality therapy is warranted because the potential benefit — a lifetime without cancer — outweighs a period of treatment harm.

**Control** means slowing or halting progression when cure is no longer attainable but the disease can be managed for months or years. Most metastatic cancer is managed with this intent. The patient lives *with* cancer, and the aim is to maintain function and buy time.

**Palliation** means relieving symptoms — pain, obstruction, bleeding, dyspnea — and improving quality of life, without necessarily extending it significantly. A bone metastasis can be irradiated to relieve pain even when the underlying disease is incurable. The treatment is judged by whether the symptom improves.

**Comfort** means that disease-modifying treatment no longer helps, and the focus shifts entirely to the patient's remaining time — hospice, symptom management, family. Continuing aggressive treatment at this stage can actively harm the patient by consuming the time and energy that remain.

The goal usually shifts over a disease course: cure, then control when cure fails, then palliation as function declines, then comfort. The critical point is that matching treatment intensity to the goal is the first clinical obligation. Aggressive multimodality therapy is the right choice for a curable cancer; it can be the wrong choice — more harm than benefit — for a patient whose goal is palliation or comfort. Treatment intent governs everything downstream, including which endpoint counts as success.

## The three modalities

Cancer treatment uses three modalities, deployed alone or in combination.

**Surgery** is the oldest and remains the most effective treatment for many localized solid tumors. The operative principles are clear margins — no tumor cells at the resection edge — adequate lymph-node sampling for accurate staging, and minimizing disruption to surrounding structures. Surgery's limitation is reach: it can remove what the surgeon can see and access, but it cannot address microscopic dissemination beyond the operative field.

**Radiation therapy** uses ionizing radiation to damage DNA and kill cells. It can be delivered as external beam therapy, brachytherapy (internal sources), or stereotactic approaches that concentrate dose precisely. Radiation is used as primary treatment for some cancers, after surgery to reduce local recurrence (adjuvant), before surgery to shrink the tumor (neoadjuvant), concurrent with chemotherapy for synergy, or for palliation of specific symptomatic sites.

**Systemic therapy** consists of drugs that travel through the bloodstream and can reach disease anywhere in the body. The major categories are **chemotherapy** (cytotoxic agents that disrupt DNA synthesis or the mitotic machinery), **hormonal therapy** (blocking hormones that drive growth, as in estrogen blockade for breast cancer or androgen deprivation for prostate), **targeted therapy** (small molecules or antibodies against specific molecular features of cancer cells — kinase inhibitors, HER2-directed antibodies), and **immunotherapy** (agents that activate the immune system against cancer — checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cell therapies).

No modern cancer treatment uses just one modality when combining them produces a better result. The rationale for combination is that each modality addresses what the others cannot: surgery removes the bulk primary tumor but leaves microscopic disease; radiation sterilizes the local bed; systemic therapy addresses distant micrometastases. Because the decisions about how to combine them involve multiple specialties, treatment is planned by **multidisciplinary tumor boards** — surgeon, radiation oncologist, medical oncologist, pathologist, and radiologist together — rather than sequentially or siloed.

---

## Timing has a name, and the name encodes the logic

When systemic therapy or radiation is given relative to definitive local treatment is not a scheduling detail — it changes what the treatment is trying to accomplish.

**Neoadjuvant** therapy is given *before* the primary intervention. Chemotherapy before breast cancer surgery can shrink the tumor, sometimes enabling a lumpectomy where mastectomy would have been required. A complete pathologic response — no residual tumor in the surgical specimen — is associated with better outcomes in some cancers and is itself an endpoint in some trials. Neoadjuvant therapy also reveals whether the cancer responds to a particular regimen before committing to the full course.

**Adjuvant** therapy is given *after* the primary intervention. The patient has had surgery or radiation and has no detectable disease. The rationale is that micrometastases — cell clusters too small to see on any imaging — are statistically likely to be present in patients whose pathology puts them at elevated risk of recurrence. Adjuvant therapy reduces the probability that those micrometastases grow into clinical metastases. The justification is population-level evidence from randomized trials: patients who receive adjuvant therapy recur less often and live longer than those who do not. You are treating disease you cannot see, on the basis of that statistical evidence.

**Concurrent** therapy means two modalities delivered simultaneously, typically chemoradiation. Platinum-based chemotherapy given concurrently with radiation in head and neck cancer or cervical cancer sensitizes tumor cells to radiation and can reach microscopic disease the radiation field does not cover. The combination is more toxic than either alone, but the synergy is established.

**Consolidation** therapy is given after primary treatment to deepen a response. **Maintenance** therapy is ongoing treatment after initial response to prevent progression — hormone therapy continued for years after early breast cancer treatment, for example.

Understanding these terms is not jargon management. Each describes a different mechanism and a different evidentiary basis. Adjuvant chemotherapy and maintenance therapy both involve giving drugs to a patient who shows no evidence of disease, but they differ in what they are trying to do, when the evidence for them was developed, and how long they continue.

## Surrogate endpoints and the proxy trap

To evaluate a treatment, you measure something. The cleanest measures — **hard endpoints** — are the outcomes patients ultimately care about: **overall survival**, how long they live, and **quality of life**, how they experience that time. Hard endpoints take years to mature in trials, so trials use **surrogate endpoints** — faster, easier proxies expected to predict the hard outcome.

The major surrogate endpoints in oncology:

**Overall response rate (ORR)** is the fraction of patients whose tumors shrink by a defined threshold. RECIST criteria define a partial response as at least a 30% reduction in the sum of target lesion diameters; complete response is disappearance of all target lesions.

**Progression-free survival (PFS)** is the time from randomization until tumor growth or death. It matures faster than OS because progression events happen before deaths.

**Biomarker response** — falling PSA, CA-125, CEA, or circulating tumor DNA — is the fastest proxy of all.

A surrogate is only trustworthy if improving it reliably improves the hard endpoint. Sometimes it does. In some hematologic malignancies, response rate predicts survival well. In some solid-tumor settings, PFS has been validated as a surrogate for OS. In many others, it has not, and the opening case is the pattern: tumors shrank and growth was delayed, but survival did not change.

The recurring failure mode in oncology evidence is treating the proxy as the thing itself. A drug that improves response rate and PFS has demonstrated that it *affects the tumor*. It has not demonstrated that it *benefits the patient* until the hard endpoints move. The FDA's accelerated-approval pathway allows initial approval on surrogate endpoints, with the understanding that confirmatory trials proving real benefit follow. Recent FDA initiatives — including Project Optimus, focusing on dose optimization for meaningful benefit — reflect awareness that approvals on surrogates have sometimes resulted in drugs that reached patients without ever demonstrating survival benefit.

The endpoint hierarchy for evaluation: quality of life and overall survival are the arbiters. PFS can stand in when its surrogate validity has been established in the specific cancer type. ORR is a hypothesis about benefit.

## Reading the opening case

Apply the endpoint hierarchy to the drug in the opening case.

**ORR 60% vs 20% (surrogate).** Strongly favors the new drug. This tells us the drug is biologically active and affects tumor biology. It does not tell us whether that activity benefits patients.

**PFS improved (surrogate).** A 3-month PFS gain is real. Whether it translates to survival depends on whether PFS is a validated OS surrogate in this particular cancer, and whether a 3-month delay in progression represents a meaningful clinical benefit. Neither is given.

**OS: no significant difference (hard endpoint).** Patients did not live longer. When both arms die at the same time, the question of whether the drug extends life — the question the patient is actually asking — is answered: it does not, in this trial.

**Grade 3–4 toxicity: higher in the new arm (quality of life, hard endpoint).** Patients on the new drug felt worse. Both hard endpoints failed to improve, and one moved in the wrong direction.

The recommendation depends on the goal. For a patient whose goal is to maximize survival, this trial provides no evidence the drug delivers it. For a patient whose goal includes avoiding toxicity, the evidence points away from it. A change in surrogates is a hypothesis about benefit, not benefit. Tumor shrinkage is what the drug does to the tumor; overall survival and quality of life are what it does for the patient, and the two can diverge — and did.

## Response assessment and its own artifacts

Once a patient is on treatment, response is measured. RECIST provides the standard categories: complete response, partial response, stable disease, and progressive disease. The categories are defined by percentage changes in the sum of target-lesion diameters on imaging.

Immunotherapy introduced a complication: **pseudoprogression**. Immune checkpoint inhibitors can cause early apparent growth — tumor lesions look larger on the first post-treatment scan — because immune cells are infiltrating and the lesion is inflamed before regression occurs. Standard RECIST applied at that point would declare the patient a non-responder and potentially discontinue a treatment that was actually working. Modified criteria, iRECIST, require confirming apparent progression at a later time point before concluding the drug failed.

This is a microcosm of the chapter's theme. Response assessment is a measurement system, and measurement systems have artifacts. An early RECIST reading in an immunotherapy patient is a proxy for the underlying biology, and that proxy can mislead at the specific timepoint when an immune response is generating transient inflammation. The underlying question — is this tumor responding? — requires the right measurement at the right time.

---

## What would change this picture

The chapter's central claim is that surrogate endpoints can move without hard endpoints following, so treatment claims must ultimately rest on overall survival and quality of life. The finding that would revise this: rigorous, disease-specific evidence that a particular surrogate is a *validated* predictor of survival — a formal meta-analysis showing that in a given cancer, improvements in PFS reliably and quantitatively translate into improvements in OS across many trials. Where such validation exists, the surrogate earns the right to stand in for the hard endpoint, and the skepticism relaxes for that setting. Conversely, continued accumulation of approved drugs that improved surrogates but never confirmed survival benefit in post-approval trials would harden the conclusion that proxies must be treated as hypotheses until the hard endpoint is in.

---

## Still open

For which cancers and which surrogates is progression-free survival actually a validated stand-in for overall survival, and for which is it not? The answer varies by disease and is often unsettled. How a clinician should act in the unsettled zone — use PFS as a surrogate where its validity is uncertain — is a recurring practical tension.

When a drug improves quality of life but not survival, or survival but not quality of life, how should those two hard endpoints be weighed against each other? Trials can be designed to test both, but they often are not, and the prioritization between length and quality of remaining life is ultimately the patient's judgment, not the trialist's.

The four-goal hierarchy treats cure and comfort as opposite ends of a spectrum, with control and palliation between them. But there is evidence — the Temel trial of early palliative care in lung cancer is the most cited — that introducing comfort-directed care early, alongside disease-modifying treatment, can improve both quality of life and survival. If comfort care can extend life, the boundary between the cure/control goals and the comfort goal is less clear than the hierarchy implies.

## LLM Exercises

1. **(Definitions)** Define neoadjuvant, adjuvant, and concurrent therapy in one sentence each. For each, state the distinct rationale in terms of what it is trying to accomplish, when it is given, and how its success would be measured.

2. **(Endpoint hierarchy)** A trial reports that Drug X improves median PFS from 6 to 9 months with no significant change in overall survival. Classify PFS as a surrogate or hard endpoint and explain why. List two scenarios in which this PFS gain is genuinely valuable to a patient and two in which it is not. State what additional data you would request before recommending the drug.

3. **(Full endpoint analysis)** A pivotal trial reports: ORR 45% vs 25%; median PFS 8 vs 5 months (p = 0.02); median OS 18 vs 17 months (p = 0.41); grade 3–4 toxicity 35% vs 20%. Build a table classifying each endpoint as surrogate or hard, mark direction and significance, and write a one-paragraph recommendation that explicitly weights the hard endpoints above the surrogates. State the treatment goal your recommendation assumes and how the recommendation would change if the goal shifted.

4. **(Treatment intent decision tree)** Draw a treatment-intent decision tree for a patient who presents with localized colorectal cancer that is successfully resected, relapses with limited liver metastases, and then develops widespread metastatic disease. At each node, identify the goal, the dominant modality, and the endpoint by which success would be judged. Mark the transition points where the goal shifts.

5. **(Accelerated approval argument)** The FDA's accelerated-approval pathway allows initial drug approval on surrogate endpoints, with confirmatory trials required afterward. Some drugs have remained approved for years without completing confirmatory trials. Argue either that this is the right trade-off or the wrong one, grounding the argument in the surrogate-versus-hard-endpoint distinction and identifying what evidence standard would resolve the disagreement.
