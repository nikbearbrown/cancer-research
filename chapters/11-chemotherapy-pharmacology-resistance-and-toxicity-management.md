# Chapter 11 — Chemotherapy: Pharmacology, Resistance, and Toxicity Management
*Why a milligram is not a milligram until it enters a specific body — and what happens when the body the drug enters metabolizes it at the wrong speed.*

Five days after starting capecitabine at the protocol dose, she is back in the emergency department. Violent diarrhea. Mouth ulcers severe enough that she cannot eat. A neutrophil count near zero. She looks like someone who received many times the intended dose.

She received exactly the dose the protocol specified.

The protocol was written for a patient with normal drug metabolism. She does not have normal drug metabolism. She carries two loss-of-function variants in *DPYD*, the gene encoding dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase — the enzyme responsible for clearing roughly 80 percent of administered fluoropyrimidine. With functional DPD, a standard capecitabine dose produces a therapeutic plasma concentration. Without functional DPD, the drug accumulates. The standard dose was, for this patient, a functional overdose, and nobody tested for the variant before the first pill.

The error was not arithmetic. Nobody miscalculated the dose. The error was conceptual: treating "the dose" as a fixed property of the drug rather than of the drug in this patient. A prescribed milligram is just intent. What the tumor and the normal tissues actually see depends on how that milligram is absorbed, where it distributes, how quickly it is metabolized, and how efficiently it is excreted — and every one of those processes varies between patients, sometimes by a factor of five or ten.

This chapter is about that variation, and about the resistance and toxicity that follow from it.

---

### What the body does to the drug: ADME

Pharmacokinetics is the study of what the body does to a drug. The acronym is ADME: absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion.

**Absorption** is how the drug gets in. Most chemotherapy is administered intravenously, bypassing the gut entirely — the drug enters the bloodstream directly and absorption variability is minimal. Oral agents are different. Capecitabine, temozolomide, and hydroxyurea must cross the gut wall, survive first-pass hepatic extraction, and do so consistently across patients with different gastrointestinal function, food intake, and concurrent medications. A patient who takes her capecitabine with a heavy meal on one day and on an empty stomach the next has delivered two different functional doses.

**Distribution** is where the drug travels. Highly protein-bound drugs are mostly tied to albumin in the blood and only the free fraction is pharmacologically active — a patient with low albumin, common in malnourished cancer patients, has more free drug than expected. Lipid-soluble drugs accumulate in fat; hydrophilic drugs stay in the aqueous compartments. The most clinically important distribution barrier is the blood-brain barrier, a tight junction system between the brain's capillaries and the central nervous system that excludes most large or hydrophilic molecules. Tumors in the brain represent a **sanctuary site** — a compartment where drug concentrations remain low even when plasma concentrations are therapeutic, allowing residual disease to persist and progress while disease elsewhere responds. This is why intrathecal chemotherapy — injecting drug directly into the cerebrospinal fluid — is used for leukemic meningitis rather than simply escalating systemic doses.

**Metabolism** transforms the drug chemically, primarily in the liver through cytochrome P450 enzymes, but also in other tissues. The clinical significance of hepatic metabolism runs in two directions. Most drugs are inactivated by metabolism and their clearance depends on hepatic function — doxorubicin is substantially cleared by the liver, and patients with elevated bilirubin or hepatic insufficiency accumulate the drug and require dose reduction or avoidance. A smaller class of drugs are **prodrugs** that require metabolic activation. Cyclophosphamide requires hepatic hydroxylation to generate phosphoramide mustard, the active alkylating species. Capecitabine is converted to 5-fluorouracil through a three-step enzymatic cascade. Irinotecan is converted in the gut and liver to SN-38, the active topoisomerase I inhibitor. For prodrugs, impaired activation as well as impaired clearance can alter the drug's effective dose.

**Excretion** is elimination. Renally cleared drugs — cisplatin, methotrexate, carboplatin — depend on glomerular filtration, and a patient with impaired renal function accumulates them. Cisplatin is directly nephrotoxic, creating a vicious cycle: the drug damages the kidney, the damaged kidney clears the drug more slowly, the higher drug concentration causes more renal damage. Methotrexate at high doses requires aggressive hydration, urinary alkalinization, and close monitoring of drug levels until clearance is confirmed. Doxorubicin is excreted in bile, so hepatic dysfunction — not renal dysfunction — requires dose adjustment. Knowing which organ clears a drug is not a pharmacology trivia question; it is the minimum requirement for safe prescribing in a patient whose organs are not functioning normally.

### Pharmacogenomics: when a gene changes everything about a dose

Pharmacogenomics is the study of how inherited genetic variation changes drug response. For a small number of chemotherapy drugs, the variation is not a modest modifier — it is the difference between a therapeutic dose and a life-threatening one.

**DPYD and fluoropyrimidines.** Dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase, encoded by *DPYD*, is responsible for the initial step in catabolism of 5-fluorouracil and its prodrugs. Roughly 3–5 percent of the population carries loss-of-function variants — most commonly DPYD\*2A, c.2846A>T, and the HapB3 haplotype — that reduce DPD activity substantially. The standard capecitabine or 5-FU dose calculated for normal-metabolizer patients accumulates to far higher concentrations in a patient with impaired DPD. The clinical consequence is what the opening case describes: severe mucositis, diarrhea, and myelosuppression. Fatalities have occurred. Pre-treatment testing for DPYD variants is now mandated before fluoropyrimidine administration in several European countries and increasingly recommended elsewhere. Dose reduction by 25–50 percent for heterozygous carriers, and avoidance or extreme caution for homozygous carriers, prevents the toxicity.

**UGT1A1 and irinotecan.** Irinotecan is a prodrug converted to its active metabolite SN-38 by carboxylesterases. SN-38 is then glucuronidated by UGT1A1 for elimination. The UGT1A1\*28 variant — a TA repeat polymorphism in the promoter that reduces UGT1A1 expression — impairs SN-38 glucuronidation. Patients homozygous for UGT1A1\*28 accumulate SN-38 and are at elevated risk for severe neutropenia and diarrhea. Genotyping before irinotecan and dose adjustment for high-risk genotypes is supported by regulatory labeling.

**TPMT and thiopurines.** Thiopurine S-methyltransferase inactivates 6-mercaptopurine and azathioprine. Loss-of-function *TPMT* variants — present in about 10 percent of the population at heterozygous frequency and 1 in 300 at homozygous frequency — reduce inactivation of the active thiopurine nucleotide pool, increasing the risk of severe, prolonged myelosuppression. TPMT genotyping or enzyme activity measurement before starting thiopurines is a well-established clinical practice.

The pharmacogenomic lesson is general: for drugs whose therapeutic index depends on a specific metabolic enzyme, the dose is not a property of the drug — it is a property of the drug *and* that enzyme's activity in the patient receiving it. Prescribing a standard dose without confirming enzyme function is not standard practice; it is an assumption about normal variation that is known to be false at clinically meaningful frequency.

### Resistance: what happens when selection pressure shapes the tumor

A cancer that responds to chemotherapy is not cured unless every cell is killed. The cells that survive treatment are, almost by definition, the ones least sensitive to it. Under the selection pressure of therapy, these cells expand, and the resulting population is more resistant than the original. This is clonal evolution applied to treatment, and it is the dominant reason cancers that initially respond eventually progress.

Resistance can be **intrinsic** — present before the first dose — or **acquired** — emerging under treatment. The mechanisms are multiple and often coexist.

**Reduced drug uptake.** Methotrexate enters cells primarily through the reduced folate carrier, a membrane transporter. Cells that downregulate or lose this transporter become resistant to methotrexate regardless of what happens downstream. This is a first-barrier mechanism: the drug never reaches its target.

**Increased drug efflux.** ATP-binding cassette transporters — ABC transporters — pump hydrophobic substrates out of cells against their concentration gradient. The most studied is P-glycoprotein, encoded by *ABCB1* (also called *MDR1*). P-glycoprotein is expressed at high levels in the gut, liver, kidney, and blood-brain barrier under normal conditions, where its job is to protect these barriers from xenobiotics. When overexpressed in cancer cells, it pumps out taxanes, vinca alkaloids, anthracyclines, and etoposide — structurally unrelated drugs — conferring resistance to several chemotherapy classes simultaneously. This is **multidrug resistance**: one efflux mechanism creates cross-resistance to multiple drugs.

The observation of P-glycoprotein-mediated resistance generated an obvious therapeutic hypothesis: block the pump and restore sensitivity. P-glycoprotein inhibitors were developed and tested in clinical trials through the 1990s and 2000s. They failed to improve outcomes. The failure was instructive on two counts. First, the inhibitors were not clean — they also inhibited normal P-glycoprotein function in the gut and liver, altering the pharmacokinetics of co-administered chemotherapy in ways that increased systemic drug concentrations and toxicity rather than selectively reversing tumor resistance. Second, and more fundamentally, resistance in clinical tumors is not a single-pump problem. It runs through multiple parallel mechanisms — other ABC transporters, enhanced DNA repair, apoptosis evasion, drug inactivation — so blocking one efflux pump leaves the others operating. The tumor had multiple routes around the block.

**Drug inactivation.** Glutathione conjugation neutralizes alkylating agents and platinum compounds. Cytidine deaminase converts cytidine analogs to inactive forms. Elevated expression of the relevant inactivating enzyme in a tumor cell removes the drug's active species before it can reach its target.

**Target alteration.** 5-Fluorouracil exerts most of its cytotoxic effect by inhibiting thymidylate synthase, the enzyme that synthesizes the thymidine needed for DNA replication. Upregulation or mutation of thymidylate synthase reduces fluoropyrimidine sensitivity. Anthracyclines and etoposide act through topoisomerase II; point mutations or expression changes in topoisomerase II that reduce drug binding confer resistance.

**Enhanced DNA repair.** Alkylating agents and platinum compounds form DNA adducts that stall replication forks. *MGMT* encodes a methyltransferase that directly removes the methyl group added by temozolomide, the alkylator used for glioblastoma; tumors with high MGMT expression clear the adducts efficiently and are less sensitive to temozolomide. Cisplatin and carboplatin form platinum-DNA crosslinks that are repaired by nucleotide excision repair; high expression of *ERCC1*, a key NER component, correlates with platinum resistance in several cancers. PARP inhibitors and platinum agents exploit homologous recombination deficiency in BRCA-mutant cancers; **reversion mutations** in BRCA that restore the reading frame and re-enable HR represent an acquired resistance mechanism seen in clinical relapse after PARP inhibitor or platinum treatment.

**Apoptosis evasion.** Chemotherapy kills cells by inducing apoptosis. *TP53* mutation removes the transcriptional response that would enact cell cycle arrest and death; *BCL-2* overexpression prevents mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization. Either change allows a cell to sustain DNA damage that should have killed it and continue dividing, passing the damage to daughter cells. The selective advantage is immediate and strong under chemotherapy pressure.

These mechanisms do not operate in isolation. A tumor cell that has reduced its drug uptake, increased efflux, and upregulated DNA repair will survive concentrations of drug that would kill cells with any one of these alterations individually. Resistance, in clinical tumors, is typically a system of overlapping mechanisms rather than a single leverage point. This is why the single-target reversal strategy — block the pump, rescue sensitivity — failed in the clinic, and why the more productive frame is to ask which combination of mechanisms is driving resistance in a specific tumor and whether any of them creates a new vulnerability.

### Toxicity: what limits delivery and how to manage it

Chemotherapy toxicity is the constraint on everything else. The dose that would reliably kill the tumor would reliably kill the patient. Actual dosing navigates between therapeutic effect and the toxicities that cause harm, and supportive care is what makes higher, more effective doses deliverable.

**Myelosuppression** is the dominant dose-limiting toxicity for most conventional chemotherapy. Bone marrow contains the rapidly dividing precursor cells that resupply the blood with neutrophils, platelets, and red cells. Chemotherapy that kills rapidly dividing cells does not distinguish between the tumor and the marrow. After a dose, the circulating mature cells — with their finite lifespans — gradually die off without replacement, and blood counts fall. The **nadir** — the lowest point — occurs roughly 7 to 14 days after most chemotherapy regimens. By day 21 or so, when the marrow has had time to recover, counts typically return toward baseline, which is why 21-day cycles are so common.

The clinical consequence that matters most is **febrile neutropenia**: fever in a patient with a neutrophil count below 500 cells per microliter. At this level, infection by normal skin and gut flora can progress to sepsis rapidly; there is no neutrophil reserve to contain it. Febrile neutropenia is a medical emergency. Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics must be started promptly, before culture results return, because delaying treatment while waiting for sensitivities costs lives. The specific antibiotic regimen, the role of antifungals, and the threshold for hospital admission versus outpatient management depend on the clinical context and the patient's risk features.

Prophylactic **G-CSF** — granulocyte colony-stimulating factor, which accelerates neutrophil recovery from marrow progenitors — reduces the depth and duration of the nadir and reduces the risk of febrile neutropenia. Pegfilgrastim, the long-acting formulation given once per cycle, simplifies administration. The indication is calibrated: G-CSF prophylaxis is recommended when the febrile-neutropenia risk from the regimen exceeds approximately 20 percent, or when the patient's individual risk factors — age, comorbidity, prior chemotherapy — raise the baseline risk to a similar level. Routine use in all patients is not supported because the drug has its own side effects (bone pain, rarely splenic rupture) and adds cost.

**Peripheral neuropathy** is the dose-limiting toxicity for platinum compounds, taxanes, vinca alkaloids, and bortezomib. It is largely cumulative — it worsens with each cycle and does not fully reverse when the drug is stopped. Patients describe numbness, tingling, burning, or pain in the hands and feet, and in severe cases loss of balance and fine motor control. There is no established effective pharmacological prevention; dose modification or discontinuation remains the primary management. The cumulative nature of neuropathy creates a tension: completing full cycles of an effective regimen may produce permanent functional impairment that outlasts the cancer.

**Nausea and vomiting** were, for much of chemotherapy's history, so severe that patients refused treatment. Cisplatin was notorious: a highly emetogenic agent whose acute nausea peaks in the first 24 hours and whose delayed nausea persists for days. The development of serotonin antagonists (5-HT3 receptor antagonists), followed by NK1 receptor antagonists, and subsequently the recognition of olanzapine's antiemetic efficacy, transformed the management of chemotherapy-induced nausea. Modern prophylactic regimens — combining all three classes with corticosteroids — have converted even highly emetogenic chemotherapy from a treatment patients refused into one most can tolerate. The key is prophylaxis before the first dose: anticipatory nausea — the conditioned nausea that develops after a bad first experience — is extremely difficult to treat once established. Allowing severe nausea on cycle one ensures worse nausea on every subsequent cycle.

### The supportive-care plan as part of the prescription

The worked example in this chapter's source material resolves to a principle worth making explicit: for any emetogenic or myelosuppressive regimen, the supportive-care plan is written before the first dose, not assembled reactively as problems arise.

For a highly emetogenic platinum-based regimen, that plan looks like this: 5-HT3 antagonist, NK1 antagonist, dexamethasone, and olanzapine given before and after each infusion, tapered over several days for the delayed phase. Pre-hydration and post-hydration with isotonic fluid for renal protection, with magnesium replacement because cisplatin causes renal magnesium wasting. A dated calendar for the patient: chemotherapy on day 1, antiemetic taper through day 4, blood count check at day 10–12 to capture the nadir, emergency instructions for fever in that window, and resumption of cycle 2 on day 22 only after count recovery is confirmed.

The calendar is not paperwork. It is the operational translation of the drug's pharmacology and toxicity profile into the patient's experience. The nadir date is derived from the drug's mechanism of action on marrow kinetics. The antiemetic schedule is derived from the drug's emetogenicity and the pharmacokinetics of delayed nausea. The hydration volume is derived from the mechanism of cisplatin nephrotoxicity. Each element of the plan is a prediction based on known biology — a prediction the care team acts on before the problem manifests.

This is the discipline the opening case lacked. Not the arithmetic of dosing, but the prior step: confirming that the patient's biology is consistent with receiving a standard dose safely, through DPYD genotyping for fluoropyrimidines, through renal function assessment for cisplatin, through liver function for doxorubicin. Then, having confirmed eligibility, writing the supportive-care plan that makes delivery of that dose survivable. The protocol number is the starting point. The plan is everything that happens after.

**Warm-up**

1. *(Recall — difficulty: low)* Define each of the four ADME steps and, for each, name one chemotherapy drug whose clinical dosing or monitoring is primarily governed by that step. Explain in one sentence each why hepatic dysfunction requires doxorubicin dose reduction but renal dysfunction requires cisplatin dose reduction. *What this tests: ADME as a framework for understanding drug-specific dosing requirements before applying it to organ-impaired patients.*

2. *(Recall — difficulty: low)* Name the three pharmacogenomic gene-drug pairs in this chapter. For each, state the gene, the enzyme it encodes, the drug(s) affected, and the specific toxicity that loss-of-function variants produce. *What this tests: the three core pharmacogenomic relationships before applying them to patient scenarios.*

3. *(Recall — difficulty: low)* What is the nadir, when does it typically occur after most chemotherapy regimens, and what is the medical emergency associated with it? State what treatment must begin immediately when a neutropenic patient develops fever, and why it cannot wait for culture results. *What this tests: nadir timing and febrile neutropenia management before the supportive-care plan is constructed.*

**Application**

4. *(Apply — difficulty: medium)* A patient with stage III colon cancer is about to start FOLFOX — oxaliplatin, leucovorin, and 5-fluorouracil — on a 14-day cycle. Before the first dose, you want to assess pharmacogenomic risk. Which gene would you test, what variant are you looking for, and what would you do if the patient returned as a heterozygous carrier? Then address the regimen's other major toxicity: oxaliplatin causes cumulative peripheral neuropathy. Explain why this toxicity is not preventable by the same prophylactic approach used for nausea, and describe how it is managed when it reaches grade 2 severity. *What this tests: DPYD testing applied to a specific regimen plus the distinct management logic for neuropathy versus emetogenic toxicity.*

5. *(Apply — difficulty: medium)* A patient's metastatic cancer responded to first-line chemotherapy for eight months, responded less impressively to second-line for four months, and barely responded to third-line. Using the resistance mechanisms in this chapter, explain this declining-response pattern as clonal evolution under selection pressure. Name at least three resistance mechanisms that could plausibly be operating in the third-line-refractory tumor, and explain why simply re-using the first-line drug at a higher dose is unlikely to rescue the situation. *What this tests: evolutionary framing of resistance and the multi-mechanism nature of clinical refractory disease.*

6. *(Apply — difficulty: medium)* A colleague proposes testing a P-glycoprotein inhibitor in combination with paclitaxel in an unselected population of taxane-resistant breast cancer patients. Using the evidence from the failed P-glycoprotein inhibitor trials, write a three-point critique of the trial design. Then propose what modification — in patient selection, in inhibitor design, or in trial endpoint — would be required to make the experiment interpretable if it failed again. *What this tests: the failed-trial history applied to a specific new proposal, and the construction of a testable hypothesis from a failed mechanism.*

**Synthesis**

7. *(Synthesize — difficulty: high)* Construct a complete pre-treatment and supportive-care plan for a patient starting cisplatin plus gemcitabine for locally advanced non-small-cell lung cancer. The plan must address: (a) pre-treatment renal function assessment and dose modification criteria; (b) prophylactic antiemetic regimen with agents and timing; (c) hydration protocol with volume and duration; (d) nadir timing and the blood count monitoring schedule; (e) G-CSF decision with explicit risk-threshold reasoning; and (f) the emergency instruction to give the patient for fever during the nadir window. For each element, state the pharmacological or biological rationale that derives it from this specific regimen's properties. *What this tests: integration of pharmacokinetics, pharmacogenomics, nadir timing, and supportive-care reasoning into a single coherent clinical plan for a specific regimen.*

8. *(Synthesize — difficulty: high)* A glioblastoma patient who initially responded to temozolomide now has progressive disease. Biopsy of the recurrent tumor shows high MGMT expression. Trace the mechanism from MGMT expression to temozolomide resistance, and explain why this resistance mechanism is fundamentally different from the P-glycoprotein mechanism in its implications for whether it can be overcome. Then evaluate: does high MGMT expression in the recurrent tumor suggest you should escalate temozolomide dose, switch to a platinum-based regimen, or pursue a different strategy entirely? Justify your reasoning with the mechanisms described in the chapter. *What this tests: mechanism-specific resistance reasoning applied to a specific clinical scenario, distinguishing repair-based from efflux-based resistance.*

**Challenge**
