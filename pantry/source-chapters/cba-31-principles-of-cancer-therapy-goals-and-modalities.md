The first question in cancer therapy is not "what should we do," but "what are we trying to accomplish."

Hold the framing. The same patient with the same cancer at the same stage can be treated very differently depending on the goal. A patient with localized colon cancer might be offered surgery with curative intent, with cure rates approaching 90 percent. The same patient with stage IV colorectal cancer might be offered chemotherapy and targeted therapy with palliative intent, aiming to extend life and maintain quality of life rather than to cure. The same patient, again, might enter hospice care if disease has progressed beyond what active treatment can meaningfully address. The interventions are different. The trade-offs are different. The success criteria are different.

The decision about what to aim for — *cure*, *control*, *palliation*, or *comfort* — is the foundation of cancer therapy. It is informed by cancer biology (what is the natural history of this disease?), clinical factors (what stage, what subtype, what biomarkers?), patient factors (age, performance status, comorbidities, values, preferences), and treatment factors (what are the realistic outcomes with available therapies, what are the toxicities?). Without clarity about the goal, treatment risks being either too aggressive (causing harm that exceeds benefit) or too passive (missing opportunities for meaningful intervention).

This chapter — the first of two on principles of cancer therapy — covers the goals of cancer treatment, the major treatment modalities (surgery, radiation, systemic therapy), and the multidisciplinary approach that integrates them. The next — 19B — covers precision oncology in detail, the framework for tailoring treatment to molecular tumor features and individual patient characteristics.

Hold the question: *given the biology of cancer and the available interventions, how do we decide what to do for any individual patient?*

## The goals of cancer treatment

Cancer treatment has four broad goals, often shifting over the course of a patient's disease.

*Cure*. Eliminating the cancer entirely so that the patient lives the rest of their life cancer-free. Curative intent treatment is appropriate when the disease is localized or has limited spread that surgery, radiation, or other modalities can eliminate. Curative outcomes are realistic in localized solid tumors (early-stage breast, lung, colorectal, prostate cancer), in some hematological cancers (some leukemias, some lymphomas), and in oligometastatic disease in certain cancers.

Curative treatment typically involves aggressive multi-modality approaches: surgery plus chemotherapy plus radiation in many contexts. The treatment is intense, with significant toxicity, but the goal justifies the cost. The success of curative treatment depends on disease characteristics (stage, biology, response to therapy) and patient characteristics (ability to tolerate the treatment).

*Control of disease*. Preventing or slowing disease progression when cure is not achievable but the disease can be managed for an extended period. This is the goal for most metastatic cancer. The patient lives with cancer, often for years, with therapy controlling the disease but not eliminating it.

The distinction between curative and disease-control intent is critical. A patient with advanced melanoma on pembrolizumab might achieve durable complete response (effectively cured). Another patient might achieve stable disease for years on the same drug, with the cancer present but not progressing. A third might progress despite therapy. The therapy approach is similar, but the outcomes — and the discussions with patients about expectations — differ.

*Palliation*. Relieving symptoms and improving quality of life without necessarily extending life substantially. Palliative treatment focuses on managing pain, bleeding, organ obstruction, dyspnea, and other symptoms. It can involve surgery (palliative resection or stenting), radiation (palliative radiation to symptomatic sites), and systemic therapy when likely to provide meaningful symptom benefit.

The integration of palliative care into oncology has been one of the major developments of the past 20 years. Studies have shown that early palliative care alongside cancer treatment improves quality of life, reduces symptom burden, and (in some cases) extends life compared to palliative care delayed until end-of-life. The integration is now standard at major cancer centers.

*Hospice and end-of-life care*. When death is anticipated within months and disease-modifying treatment is no longer meaningfully helping, the focus shifts entirely to comfort and quality. Hospice care provides intensive symptom management and psychosocial support. The decision to transition to hospice involves the patient, family, and healthcare team and is often delayed inappropriately because of cultural avoidance of death-related discussions.

The goal of treatment shifts over the course of disease. Cure may be the initial goal; if cure is not achieved, control becomes the goal; eventually, palliation; finally, comfort. Skilled oncology involves recognizing these transitions and adjusting accordingly. The communication of these shifts requires honesty, sensitivity, and respect for patient autonomy.

## The treatment modalities

Cancer treatment uses three major modalities, often in combination.

*Surgery*. The oldest cancer treatment and still the most effective for many localized solid tumors. Surgical removal of the cancer is curative for many stage I and stage II solid cancers. Even when not curative, surgery can debulk disease, relieve obstruction, or address complications.

The principles of cancer surgery include obtaining clear margins (no tumor at the resection edge), adequate lymphadenectomy for staging when relevant, and minimal disturbance of surrounding structures consistent with adequate cancer removal. Modern cancer surgery has evolved toward less radical approaches (lumpectomy versus mastectomy in breast cancer, sphincter-sparing surgery in rectal cancer) without compromising oncologic outcomes.

Surgical oncology is covered in detail in Chapter 20.

*Radiation therapy*. Ionizing radiation kills cancer cells through DNA damage and apoptosis. Radiation is used:
- As primary treatment for some cancers (head and neck cancers, some skin cancers, some prostate cancers, some early-stage lung cancers).
- As adjuvant treatment after surgery (breast cancer, head and neck cancer, sarcoma, others).
- As neoadjuvant treatment before surgery (rectal cancer, some others).
- For palliation (bone metastases, brain metastases, symptomatic local disease).
- As consolidation after systemic therapy (some lymphomas).

Modern radiation oncology uses sophisticated technology (IMRT, IGRT, SBRT, proton therapy) to deliver high doses to tumors while sparing normal tissue. The technology is covered in detail in Chapter 21.

*Systemic therapy*. Drugs that travel through the bloodstream and affect cancer cells anywhere in the body. The major systemic therapy categories:

- *Chemotherapy*. Cytotoxic drugs that interfere with DNA synthesis, mitosis, or other cellular processes. The classical modality, dating from nitrogen mustards in the 1940s. Still backbone for many cancer regimens.

- *Hormonal therapy*. Drugs that interfere with hormones that drive cancer growth. Estrogen receptor inhibition for hormone-receptor-positive breast cancer. Androgen receptor inhibition for prostate cancer. These are among the oldest and most successful targeted therapies.

- *Targeted therapy*. Small molecules and antibodies directed at specific molecular features of cancer cells. EGFR inhibitors, BRAF inhibitors, HER2-targeted antibodies, BCR-ABL inhibitors, and many others. The therapeutic landscape has been transformed by these agents over the past 25 years.

- *Immunotherapy*. Drugs and cellular therapies that activate the immune system against cancer. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (PD-1, PD-L1, CTLA-4 antibodies), CAR-T cells, cancer vaccines. Covered in detail in Chapter 16.

- *Biological response modifiers*. Older immunotherapies (interferons, interleukins) and certain antibody therapies.

The choice of systemic therapy depends on cancer type, stage, molecular profile, prior treatments, and patient factors. Modern oncology increasingly uses combinations — chemotherapy plus immunotherapy, targeted therapy plus immunotherapy, sequential or concurrent multi-drug regimens.

## The multidisciplinary approach

Cancer care is intrinsically multidisciplinary. A single cancer patient may be treated by surgeons, medical oncologists, radiation oncologists, pathologists, radiologists, palliative care specialists, primary care physicians, nurses, social workers, nutritionists, physical therapists, and psychologists. The coordination of these providers is essential for good outcomes.

The *tumor board* or *multidisciplinary case conference* is the institutional embodiment of multidisciplinary cancer care. The structure varies by institution but typically involves regular meetings where new patients and challenging cases are presented and treatment plans are discussed by the relevant specialists.

A typical tumor board for a breast cancer case might involve:
- *Pathologist* presenting the biopsy findings and histology.
- *Radiologist* reviewing the imaging.
- *Surgeon* discussing surgical options and timing.
- *Medical oncologist* discussing systemic therapy options.
- *Radiation oncologist* discussing radiation indications.
- *Pathologist* providing molecular profiling results.
- *Genetic counselor* discussing hereditary cancer assessment.
- *Plastic surgeon* discussing reconstruction options.

The discussion arrives at a coordinated plan that integrates all the modalities and accounts for patient preferences.

The multidisciplinary approach reduces variability in care, ensures comprehensive consideration of all options, and improves outcomes. The institutional infrastructure to support multidisciplinary care (cancer centers with co-located specialty services, tumor board meeting time, electronic health record support for coordinated care) is one of the major investments cancer centers make.

In settings without formal tumor boards (smaller hospitals, rural settings, low- and middle-income countries), the coordination must happen through individual physician-to-physician communication. Telehealth and remote multidisciplinary review are increasingly used to extend multidisciplinary expertise to settings without on-site specialists.

## Curative-intent multimodality therapy

For many localized solid cancers, curative treatment involves multiple modalities used in sequence or combination. The sequencing matters; different sequences may produce different outcomes.

*Neoadjuvant therapy* is treatment given before the primary intervention. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy before surgery, for instance, can shrink the tumor (making surgery more conservative) and provide information about chemosensitivity (predicting response of any residual cells).

*Adjuvant therapy* is treatment given after the primary intervention to reduce recurrence risk. Adjuvant chemotherapy after surgery for colon cancer or breast cancer reduces the risk of disease recurrence by killing micrometastatic disease too small to detect.

*Concurrent therapy* combines modalities simultaneously. Concurrent chemoradiation in head and neck cancer, lung cancer, and rectal cancer combines the local effect of radiation with the systemic effect of chemotherapy, often with synergistic activity.

*Consolidation therapy* is treatment given after primary therapy to deepen response. Consolidation radiation after chemotherapy in some lymphomas. Consolidation chemotherapy after induction in some leukemias.

*Maintenance therapy* is ongoing treatment given after initial response to prevent progression. Maintenance trastuzumab after adjuvant chemotherapy in HER2-positive breast cancer. Maintenance PARP inhibitor after initial chemotherapy in BRCA-mutant ovarian cancer.

The sequencing of multimodality therapy is one of the most heavily researched aspects of cancer care. Trials over decades have established the optimal sequences for many cancers. The current standards are summarized in clinical guidelines (NCCN guidelines in the US, ESMO guidelines in Europe, individual country guidelines elsewhere) that synthesize the evidence and recommend approaches.

A typical curative-intent treatment for breast cancer might look like:
1. *Diagnosis*. Mammogram → biopsy → pathology and molecular profiling.
2. *Neoadjuvant systemic therapy* (in some cases) — chemotherapy plus targeted therapy if HER2-positive, plus immunotherapy if triple-negative.
3. *Surgery* — lumpectomy or mastectomy with sentinel lymph node biopsy or axillary dissection.
4. *Adjuvant radiation* — to the breast or chest wall and (sometimes) regional lymph nodes.
5. *Adjuvant systemic therapy* — chemotherapy (if not given neoadjuvantly), hormonal therapy (for HR-positive), HER2-targeted therapy (for HER2-positive), immunotherapy (for some triple-negative).
6. *Surveillance* for many years.

The plan is individualized based on stage, biology, response to neoadjuvant therapy, and patient factors. The integration of modalities is what produces the high cure rates seen in modern breast cancer treatment (compared to historical outcomes).

## Treatment response assessment

Once treatment begins, the response is assessed periodically. The standard framework is *RECIST (Response Evaluation Criteria In Solid Tumors)*, used in clinical trials and increasingly in routine care.

RECIST defines:
- *Complete response (CR)*. Disappearance of all target lesions.
- *Partial response (PR)*. At least 30 percent reduction in sum of target lesion diameters.
- *Stable disease (SD)*. Neither sufficient shrinkage to qualify for PR nor sufficient growth to qualify for PD.
- *Progressive disease (PD)*. At least 20 percent increase in sum of target lesion diameters, appearance of new lesions, or clinical deterioration.

Imaging is typically performed every 2-3 months during active treatment, with the specific schedule depending on cancer type and treatment.

For immunotherapy, the standard RECIST criteria can be misleading because of *pseudoprogression* — initial increase in tumor size due to immune cell infiltration before subsequent regression. Modified criteria (iRECIST, immune-related RECIST) require confirmation of progression at a second time point.

For some hematological cancers (lymphomas), PET-based response criteria (Deauville criteria, Lugano criteria) are used because FDG-PET captures metabolic response better than anatomic measurement.

Beyond imaging, response assessment includes:
- *Symptomatic response* — improvement in cancer-related symptoms.
- *Biomarker response* — declining tumor marker levels (PSA, CA-125, others).
- *Quality of life measures*.
- *Performance status assessment*.
- *Pathological response* (for neoadjuvant therapy) — assessed at the time of surgery.

The integration of these assessments determines whether to continue current therapy, switch to a different regimen, or modify treatment based on response.

## Toxicity and supportive care

Cancer treatments have significant toxicities. Managing toxicity is essential for completing planned therapy and maintaining quality of life.

*Common chemotherapy toxicities* include myelosuppression (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia), nausea and vomiting, mucositis, alopecia, fatigue, and organ-specific effects depending on the drug. Modern supportive care uses growth factors (filgrastim, pegfilgrastim) for neutropenia, antiemetics (5-HT3 antagonists, NK1 antagonists, dexamethasone) for nausea, mucositis prevention, and other approaches.

*Targeted therapy toxicities* vary by drug. Many cause skin rashes, hypertension, diarrhea, hand-foot syndrome, hepatotoxicity, or cardiac effects. Managing these often requires specific interventions and sometimes dose modifications.

*Immunotherapy toxicities* — the immune-related adverse events discussed in Chapter 16B — span many organ systems and require corticosteroid treatment, sometimes additional immunosuppression, and sometimes permanent treatment discontinuation.

*Radiation toxicities* depend on the field irradiated. Acute side effects include skin reactions, mucositis (if mucosal surfaces in field), fatigue, nausea (abdominal/pelvic radiation), pneumonitis (chest radiation). Late effects can include fibrosis, secondary malignancies, organ dysfunction.

*Surgical complications* depend on the procedure. Wound complications, postoperative infections, deep vein thrombosis, anesthesia-related complications, and procedure-specific complications.

The integration of supportive care — including symptom management, psychosocial support, nutrition, physical therapy, and palliative care — is essential to good cancer outcomes. Patients who can complete their planned therapy with manageable toxicity have better outcomes than those whose therapy is curtailed by toxicity.

*Cancer-related fatigue* is the most common and underappreciated symptom in cancer patients. It is multifactorial (anemia, inflammation, deconditioning, sleep disruption, depression, treatment effects) and requires multidisciplinary management.

*Pain management* in cancer is critical and increasingly recognized as inadequate in many settings. Opioid-based analgesia, adjunctive agents (gabapentinoids, antidepressants), procedural interventions (nerve blocks, neurolysis), and palliative radiation all contribute. The opioid epidemic has complicated pain management — appropriate use of opioids for cancer pain remains essential but is sometimes underprescribed due to over-correction against the opioid epidemic.

## Patient-centered decision-making

The ultimate decision about cancer treatment belongs to the patient, informed by the clinical team. Modern oncology emphasizes *shared decision-making* — collaborative discussions between physicians and patients that explore the clinical options, the patient's values and preferences, and the trade-offs involved in different approaches.

The framework for shared decision-making in oncology:

*Inform*. The physician explains the diagnosis, prognosis, treatment options, expected outcomes, and likely toxicities for each option. The information must be presented in language and format the patient can understand.

*Explore values*. What matters most to this patient? Length of life? Quality of life? Avoiding specific toxicities? Maintaining ability to work, travel, care for family? Religious or cultural considerations? The values shape the trade-offs each patient is willing to make.

*Identify preferences*. Given the options and the patient's values, which approach makes most sense? Does the patient want maximum treatment intensity, intermediate approach, or palliation-focused care?

*Make the decision*. The team and patient arrive at a treatment plan that incorporates both clinical considerations and patient preferences.

*Iterate*. As treatment proceeds and disease evolves, the decisions are revisited. Responses, toxicities, and changing circumstances may prompt revision of the plan.

The shared decision-making framework is sometimes difficult to implement in practice. Patients often have limited medical knowledge, are dealing with the emotional impact of a cancer diagnosis, may have unrealistic expectations, and may defer to physician recommendations rather than expressing their own preferences. Effective shared decision-making requires physicians to elicit preferences explicitly, to present information in balanced ways, and to recognize when patient understanding is incomplete.

*Decision aids* — formal tools that help patients understand options and clarify preferences — have been developed for several cancer contexts (early-stage breast cancer surgery decisions, prostate cancer treatment decisions, end-of-life decisions). The evidence on decision aids is mixed, but the tools can be helpful in many contexts.

The integration of patient-reported outcomes (PROs) into clinical care is increasingly recognized as essential. Patients' own reports of symptoms, function, and quality of life provide information that clinical assessment alone misses. Several systematic approaches to incorporate PROs into cancer care (PRO-CTCAE, PROMIS measures) are being implemented.

## What this chapter gives you

Cancer therapy operates with four broad goals — cure, control, palliation, and comfort — and these goals shift over the course of a patient's disease. Clarity about the current goal is foundational to all subsequent decisions about treatment intensity and approach.

The major treatment modalities are surgery, radiation therapy, and systemic therapy (chemotherapy, hormonal therapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy). Each has its own role; many cancers benefit from combinations of modalities used in specific sequences.

The multidisciplinary approach to cancer care — coordinated by tumor boards, integrating multiple specialty perspectives — has become the standard for comprehensive cancer treatment. The institutional infrastructure to support multidisciplinary care is one of the defining features of modern cancer centers.

Curative-intent multimodality therapy uses combinations of surgery, radiation, and systemic therapy in specific sequences (neoadjuvant, adjuvant, concurrent, consolidation, maintenance) to maximize cure rates. The sequencing matters and is informed by extensive clinical trial evidence summarized in clinical guidelines.

Treatment response assessment using RECIST and modified criteria provides standardized frameworks for evaluating cancer response to therapy. The assessment guides decisions about continuing, modifying, or changing treatment.

Toxicity management and supportive care are essential to allowing patients to complete planned therapy and to maintaining quality of life. The integration of palliative care alongside active treatment has been one of the major developments of the past 20 years.

Shared decision-making — collaborative decisions between physicians and patients informed by clinical evidence and patient values — is the framework for patient-centered cancer care. The integration of patient preferences into clinical decision-making requires skill, time, and appropriate tools.

Chapter 19B continues with precision oncology — the framework for tailoring cancer therapy to specific molecular features of individual tumors and patients, the biomarker-driven treatment paradigms that have emerged, and the implementation challenges of making precision oncology accessible.
