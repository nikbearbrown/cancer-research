Surgery is the oldest cancer treatment and still the most effective for cancer that has stayed in one place.

Hold the comparison. Most metastatic cancer cannot be cured. The drugs we have — chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy — extend life, sometimes dramatically, but cure of widespread metastatic disease remains rare. Localized cancer is different. A breast tumor confined to the breast can be cut out, and 90 to 95 percent of those patients are cured. A colon cancer confined to the colon wall: similar cure rates. A localized renal cell carcinoma, removed with margins: cured in most cases. The disease, once localized, is solved by surgical excision in a way that systemic treatment cannot match.

This is why screening matters (Chapter 17B). A cancer detected at stage I is operable. A cancer detected at stage IV usually is not. The screening transforms cancer from a systemic disease problem (hard) to a local disease problem (often soluble by surgery).

The surgical principles are old in concept but modern in technique. Halsted articulated the principles of cancer surgery in the 1880s — wide local excision with regional lymph node dissection to remove the cancer and the lymphatic drainage that might harbor microscopic disease. The Halsted radical mastectomy was the standard for breast cancer for nearly a century. Surgical oncology has since refined toward less radical procedures (lumpectomy, breast-conserving surgery) when the cancer biology permits, and toward more sophisticated technology (minimally invasive surgery, robotic surgery) that achieves the same oncologic outcomes with reduced morbidity.

This chapter — the first of two on surgical oncology — covers the principles of cancer surgery, the categories of surgical procedures, the management of surgical margins and lymph nodes, and the integration of surgery with other modalities. The next — 20B — covers minimally invasive surgery, robotic surgery, and reconstructive surgery — the modern technologies that have refined how cancer surgery is delivered.

## The principles of cancer surgery

Cancer surgery differs from other surgery in several specific ways. The differences are codified in the principles of *surgical oncology*.

*Adequate margins*. The tumor must be removed with a surrounding rim of normal tissue to ensure that no cancer cells are left behind. The required margin depends on the cancer type and the biology. For some cancers (basal cell carcinoma), millimeters of margin suffice. For others (sarcomas, pancreatic cancer), centimeters may be required. The decision is informed by the tumor's biology and the consequences of removing more tissue.

*Regional lymph node assessment*. Most carcinomas spread initially through regional lymph nodes (the drainage from the tumor site). Assessment of these nodes, either by sentinel lymph node biopsy (sampling the first nodes that drain the tumor) or by formal regional lymphadenectomy, is essential for staging and sometimes for therapy. The information from nodes determines stage and often guides adjuvant therapy decisions.

*Tumor-cell containment*. The surgeon must minimize the spillage or dispersal of cancer cells during surgery. Surgical handling of the tumor should avoid disrupting the tumor capsule, avoid contaminating the surgical field with tumor cells, and minimize the manipulation that could push cells into the bloodstream. Modern techniques (no-touch surgical approach, en bloc resection of contiguous structures) aim to achieve this.

*En bloc resection*. When tumor is adherent to or invading adjacent structures, the surgeon removes the tumor and adjacent involved structures in one piece (en bloc) rather than dissecting them apart. This prevents transection of tumor and the dissemination of cancer cells.

*Anatomic principles*. Cancer surgery follows specific anatomic boundaries that respect lymphatic drainage and embryologic fascial planes. For colon cancer, complete mesocolic excision removes the colon along with its lymphatic drainage to the supplying vessel. For rectal cancer, total mesorectal excision removes the rectum along with the mesorectum that contains the local lymphatic drainage.

*Multidisciplinary planning*. The surgeon collaborates with medical and radiation oncologists to determine the optimal sequence and combination of treatments. Neoadjuvant therapy (before surgery) may shrink the tumor, making surgery more conservative. Adjuvant therapy (after surgery) addresses microscopic residual disease.

These principles distinguish cancer surgery from other operations. A surgeon removing a benign tumor can focus on adequate gross resection. A surgeon removing a malignant tumor must additionally consider lymphatic drainage, microscopic margins, tumor-cell handling, and integration with other modalities.

## Categories of cancer surgery

Cancer operations serve different purposes. The category of operation determines the goal and the surgical approach.

*Diagnostic surgery*. Biopsy procedures to obtain tissue for diagnosis. Excisional biopsy (removing the entire lesion), incisional biopsy (removing part of a larger lesion), or sentinel lymph node biopsy. The procedure aims to obtain adequate tissue without compromising subsequent definitive treatment.

*Staging surgery*. Procedures to assess the extent of disease before definitive treatment. Laparoscopy or laparotomy for staging of intra-abdominal cancers. Mediastinoscopy for lung cancer staging. The information guides treatment planning.

*Curative resection*. The intent is removal of all cancer with the possibility of cure. This is the most common cancer operation. The procedure achieves clear margins, addresses regional nodes, and removes the cancer with minimal risk of leaving disease behind.

*Palliative surgery*. Procedures to relieve symptoms or address complications even when cure is not possible. Examples include intestinal bypass to relieve obstruction, palliative resection of a bleeding tumor, decompressive surgery for spinal cord compression.

*Reconstructive surgery*. Restoration of form and function after curative resection. Breast reconstruction after mastectomy. Free flap reconstruction after head and neck cancer surgery. Skin and soft tissue reconstruction after wide excision of sarcomas.

*Prophylactic surgery*. Removal of organs at high risk for cancer. Prophylactic mastectomy in BRCA mutation carriers. Prophylactic colectomy in familial adenomatous polyposis. Prophylactic thyroidectomy in MEN2 (RET mutation carriers). The procedure prevents cancer that would otherwise have a high probability of occurring.

*Salvage surgery*. Surgery for cancer that has progressed after primary nonsurgical treatment. Salvage cystectomy after failed bladder-preserving therapy. Salvage prostatectomy after failed radiation therapy. The procedure provides a second chance at cure but is often more morbid than initial surgery would have been.

*Metastasectomy*. Resection of metastatic disease. This is increasingly performed for oligometastatic disease in colorectal cancer (especially liver and lung metastases), renal cell carcinoma, and certain other cancers. Long-term survival is possible in selected patients with limited metastatic burden.

## Margin management

The requirement for clear margins is a core principle of cancer surgery. The implementation varies by cancer type.

*Microscopic margins (R0)*. No tumor at the cut edge of the resected specimen, as assessed by pathology. This is the goal for most cancer surgery. R0 resection is associated with the best outcomes.

*Microscopic involvement (R1)*. Tumor at the microscopic margin but no gross residual disease. This may prompt re-excision, adjuvant therapy, or close surveillance, depending on the cancer type and clinical context.

*Macroscopic residual disease (R2)*. Visible tumor left behind. This requires additional treatment — re-resection if possible, otherwise adjuvant therapy.

The width of acceptable margin varies by cancer type:
- *Breast cancer*. "No tumor on ink" (essentially R0) is now the standard for invasive disease. Wider margins were once required but the evidence supports R0 as adequate.
- *Melanoma*. 1-2 cm margins for invasive melanoma, depending on thickness.
- *Sarcomas*. 1-2 cm margins or one anatomic plane, depending on histology.
- *Colon cancer*. 5 cm proximal and distal margins.
- *Rectal cancer*. 2 cm distal margin (lower if low rectal tumor).
- *Pancreatic cancer*. Margin definition is complex; R0 includes a 1 mm clearance from tumor.
- *Glioblastoma*. Maximal safe resection is the goal; truly negative margins are impossible because the tumor infiltrates beyond the visible mass.

*Intraoperative pathological assessment* can help achieve clear margins. Frozen section analysis during surgery allows the surgeon to confirm clear margins before closing. Mohs micrographic surgery (used for skin cancers in critical areas) examines all margins during surgery, removing additional tissue as needed until clear margins are achieved.

The trade-off in margin management is between adequate cancer removal and preservation of function. Wider margins reduce cancer recurrence but may produce worse functional outcomes (more tissue removed, more reconstructive challenges, more morbidity). The balance is informed by the cancer biology and the available reconstruction options.

## Lymph node management

Most carcinomas metastasize first through regional lymph nodes. Assessment and management of lymph nodes is essential for staging and sometimes for therapy.

*Sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB)*. The technique identifies the first one or few lymph nodes that drain a tumor (the *sentinel nodes*). The sentinel nodes are removed and pathologically examined. If they contain cancer, additional nodal dissection may be performed; if they are clear, the patient is spared the morbidity of full lymphadenectomy.

SLNB was developed for melanoma and breast cancer in the 1990s and has become standard for these and several other cancers. The technique involves injecting a radiotracer (typically technetium-99m) and/or blue dye around the tumor; the tracer migrates to the sentinel nodes, which are then identified and removed.

For breast cancer, SLNB has dramatically reduced the morbidity of axillary surgery. Full axillary lymph node dissection (ALND), which produces significant arm lymphedema in 10-20 percent of patients, is now reserved for patients with significant nodal disease at SLNB. For most early-stage breast cancer with negative sentinel nodes (or minimal involvement in some recent studies), SLNB alone is sufficient.

For melanoma, SLNB provides staging information and historically guided completion lymph node dissection. Recent trials (MSLT-II) have shown that completion lymphadenectomy does not improve survival in most patients with positive sentinel nodes; close observation is now often preferred.

*Formal lymphadenectomy*. Removal of the regional lymph node basin, performed when extensive nodal involvement is known or suspected. The procedure provides staging information and may improve local control.

The extent of lymphadenectomy varies by cancer:
- *Breast cancer*. Levels I and II axillary lymph nodes (sometimes level III for extensive disease).
- *Gastric cancer*. D1 (perigastric nodes) or D2 (extended including hepatic artery, splenic artery nodes) lymphadenectomy.
- *Colon cancer*. Mesenteric nodes along the supplying vessel.
- *Lung cancer*. Mediastinal lymphadenectomy or systematic sampling.

The therapeutic benefit of lymphadenectomy beyond staging is debated for many cancers. The TROG-RTOG trials and several others have shown that more extensive lymphadenectomy doesn't necessarily improve survival beyond what's achieved with adequate staging and adjuvant therapy. The morbidity of extensive lymphadenectomy (lymphedema, surgical complications) is real.

The trend in modern surgical oncology is toward more limited lymphadenectomy based on actual risk, with adjuvant therapy used to address microscopic disease that may remain.

## The role of surgery across the cancer continuum

Surgery's role in cancer care varies based on stage and biology.

*Early-stage cancer*. Surgery is often the primary curative treatment. Stage I breast cancer (lumpectomy + radiation), stage I-II colon cancer (resection), stage I-II lung cancer (lobectomy), stage I-III melanoma (wide excision + SLNB), stage I prostate cancer (prostatectomy, often as one option). The five-year survival rates with optimal surgery in these settings are 80-95 percent.

*Locally advanced cancer*. Surgery is part of multimodality therapy. Neoadjuvant therapy may shrink the tumor (rectal cancer with chemoradiation, breast cancer with chemotherapy); surgery follows when the tumor is more resectable. Adjuvant therapy after surgery addresses microscopic residual disease.

*Metastatic cancer*. Surgery is less central but plays roles. Oligometastatic disease may benefit from metastasectomy. Symptomatic primary tumors may need palliative resection. Local control of progressing disease at specific sites may benefit from surgery. The increasing recognition that local treatment of metastases (surgery or stereotactic radiation) can extend life in selected patients has reframed the role of surgery in stage IV disease.

*Recurrent cancer*. Salvage surgery for cancers that recur after primary nonsurgical treatment. Selected patients can be cured with salvage approaches.

*Prophylactic surgery in hereditary cancer*. As described in 17A, prophylactic mastectomy, salpingo-oophorectomy, colectomy, and thyroidectomy for high-risk hereditary cancer syndromes.

The integration with other modalities is critical. The decision about timing of surgery — primary, neoadjuvant, adjuvant — depends on cancer type, stage, response to therapy, and patient factors. The decision about extent of surgery — radical versus conservative — depends on cancer biology and reconstruction options.

## Patient selection and risk assessment

Not every cancer patient is a surgical candidate. The decision involves both cancer factors and patient factors.

*Cancer factors*. Is the cancer localized enough that surgery can achieve cure or meaningful control? Are there metastases that would make surgery futile? Has the cancer responded to neoadjuvant therapy in ways that change the surgical plan? What is the histology and grade?

*Patient factors*. Can the patient tolerate the planned surgery and recovery? Specific assessments include:
- *Performance status* (ECOG, Karnofsky scales). A measure of functional capacity. Patients with poor performance status (ECOG 3-4) often cannot tolerate major surgery.
- *Cardiopulmonary reserve*. Cardiac evaluation, pulmonary function testing, cardiopulmonary exercise testing.
- *Nutritional status*. Malnutrition is associated with worse surgical outcomes; preoperative nutrition optimization may be needed.
- *Comorbidities*. Diabetes, kidney disease, liver disease, COPD all affect surgical risk.
- *Age*. Not an absolute contraindication, but advanced age combined with comorbidities affects risk.
- *Frailty*. Multidimensional assessment of physiological reserve. Frail patients have higher complication rates.

The American College of Surgeons National Surgical Quality Improvement Program (NSQIP) has developed risk calculators that estimate surgical morbidity and mortality based on patient characteristics. The information helps with patient counseling and decision-making.

*Prehabilitation* — interventions to optimize patient status before surgery — is increasingly used. Components include exercise training, nutrition optimization, smoking cessation, alcohol reduction, anemia correction, and management of comorbidities. The evidence for prehabilitation reducing postoperative complications is moderate and growing.

The decision about whether to operate involves weighing the expected oncologic benefit against the surgical risks. For low-risk surgery on otherwise healthy patients, the benefit clearly outweighs the risk. For high-risk surgery on patients with significant comorbidities, the calculation requires careful consideration. Surgery that achieves cure but leaves the patient debilitated is often not preferred over less aggressive treatment that preserves quality of life.

## Surgical complications and management

Cancer surgery is generally safe but not risk-free. The complications vary by procedure but include:

*General surgical complications*. Bleeding, infection, anesthesia-related events, deep vein thrombosis, pneumonia. Modern perioperative care (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery protocols) has reduced many of these.

*Wound complications*. Surgical site infections, wound dehiscence, hernia formation.

*Specific procedure complications*:
- *Lymphedema* after extensive lymphadenectomy. Common after axillary lymph node dissection for breast cancer.
- *Anastomotic leak* after GI surgery, especially low rectal resections, esophagectomy, pancreatic resections.
- *Erectile dysfunction and incontinence* after prostatectomy.
- *Pulmonary complications* after thoracic surgery.
- *Speech and swallowing problems* after head and neck cancer surgery.
- *Pancreatic fistula* after pancreatic surgery.
- *Bile leak* after liver surgery.

*Long-term sequelae*:
- *Functional limitations*. After major resections, function may be permanently altered.
- *Body image issues*. Especially after mastectomy, head and neck surgery, ostomy creation.
- *Lymphedema* persistence and progression.
- *Adhesions* causing later bowel obstruction.
- *Hernias* at incision sites.
- *Late complications of specific procedures*.

The integration of surgery with other modalities can affect complication patterns. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy or radiation may increase wound healing problems. Adjuvant therapy must wait until adequate recovery from surgery.

*Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS)* protocols have been developed for various cancer surgeries. The protocols standardize perioperative care to optimize recovery: early mobilization, early refeeding, multimodal analgesia (reducing opioid use), goal-directed fluid therapy, normothermia maintenance, and several other elements. ERAS implementation has shortened hospital stays and reduced complications.

## What this chapter gives you

Surgery is the oldest cancer treatment and remains the most effective approach for localized cancer. The principles of cancer surgery — adequate margins, regional lymph node assessment, tumor-cell containment, en bloc resection of involved structures, anatomic resection along defined planes, and multidisciplinary planning — distinguish cancer operations from other surgery.

The categories of cancer surgery include diagnostic (biopsy), staging, curative resection, palliative surgery, reconstructive surgery, prophylactic surgery, salvage surgery, and metastasectomy. Each serves a different purpose and has different surgical considerations.

Margin management is a core principle. R0 (microscopic clear margins) is the goal for most cancer surgery. The required margin width varies by cancer type. Intraoperative pathological assessment can help achieve clear margins.

Lymph node management balances staging accuracy against operative morbidity. Sentinel lymph node biopsy has dramatically reduced the morbidity of nodal assessment for breast cancer and melanoma. Extensive lymphadenectomy is performed when extensive disease is known or suspected; the therapeutic benefit beyond staging is debated for many cancers.

Surgery's role varies across the cancer continuum, from primary curative treatment for early-stage cancer through multimodality therapy for locally advanced disease, oligometastatic surgery in stage IV, salvage surgery for recurrence, and prophylactic surgery in hereditary cancer.

Patient selection considers both cancer factors and patient factors. Performance status, cardiopulmonary reserve, nutritional status, comorbidities, age, and frailty all affect surgical risk. Prehabilitation interventions can optimize patient status before surgery.

Surgical complications are minimized through modern perioperative care and ERAS protocols. The complications vary by procedure and include wound issues, lymphedema, anastomotic problems, organ-specific complications, and functional sequelae.

Chapter 20B turns to the modern technologies that have refined cancer surgery — minimally invasive approaches, robotic surgery, and reconstructive surgery. The technologies achieve the same oncologic outcomes with reduced morbidity and improved functional results.
