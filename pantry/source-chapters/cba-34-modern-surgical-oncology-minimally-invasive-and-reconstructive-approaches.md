The best surgery is the surgery the patient barely notices.

Hold the framing. For most of cancer surgery's history, the operation required large incisions, prolonged recovery, significant pain, and substantial functional impact. The patient was effectively bedridden for days to weeks. The body bore visible scars. The disruption to work, family, and daily life was profound. The oncologic outcome was sometimes excellent — cancers removed, lives saved — but the cost in patient suffering was high.

Modern surgical oncology has pursued a different paradigm. Achieve the same oncologic outcomes with smaller incisions, less tissue disruption, shorter recovery, less pain, and better functional outcomes. The pursuit has produced *minimally invasive surgery* (laparoscopy, thoracoscopy, robotic surgery), *image-guided procedures* (interventional radiology, focal ablation), and *reconstructive surgery* that restores form and function after necessary resection. The combined effect is that cancer surgery in 2026 looks very different from cancer surgery in 1990, with substantially better patient experience and similar or improved oncologic outcomes.

This chapter — the second on surgical oncology — covers the modern technologies that have refined cancer surgery: laparoscopic and thoracoscopic surgery, robotic surgery, focal ablation, sentinel lymph node biopsy and image-guided lymphatic mapping, and reconstructive surgery after cancer resection. The chapter integrates with 20A, which covered the principles of cancer surgery generally.

Hold the question: *how have modern surgical technologies preserved or improved oncologic outcomes while reducing the patient's burden of surgery?*

## Minimally invasive surgery: the basic concept

Minimally invasive surgery (MIS) accomplishes operations through small incisions using specialized instruments and visualization through cameras inserted into body cavities. The category includes:

*Laparoscopic surgery*. Operations in the abdominal cavity through small (5-12 mm) incisions. The abdomen is insufflated with carbon dioxide to create working space. A laparoscope (camera) inserted through one port provides visualization on a monitor; specialized instruments inserted through other ports allow manipulation.

*Thoracoscopic surgery (VATS — Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery)*. Operations in the chest through small incisions, using a camera and specialized instruments. The lung is typically deflated for visualization.

*Robotic surgery*. A robot-assisted approach where the surgeon controls instruments remotely through a console. The robot (typically the da Vinci system in current practice) provides three-dimensional visualization, articulated instruments with greater dexterity than straight laparoscopic instruments, and ergonomic positioning for the surgeon.

The basic advantages of MIS over open surgery:
- *Smaller incisions* with less tissue disruption.
- *Less postoperative pain*.
- *Shorter hospital stays*.
- *Faster recovery and return to normal activity*.
- *Reduced wound complications*.
- *Better cosmetic outcomes*.

The concerns about MIS for cancer:
- *Visualization*. Less direct tactile and visual feedback than open surgery. Modern cameras and 3D systems have addressed much of this.
- *Tumor handling*. Potential for tumor cell dispersion during manipulation. Standardized techniques (no-touch, en bloc principles) have addressed concerns.
- *Margin assessment*. Slightly more challenging to assess margins intraoperatively. Pathology review remains the standard.
- *Port-site recurrences*. Initially a concern (tumor cells implanting at port incisions), now uncommon with standardized techniques.

The oncologic outcomes question — whether MIS produces equivalent cancer outcomes to open surgery — has been answered by multiple randomized trials in different cancer types. The general answer: equivalent in most contexts, with the caveats discussed below.

## Laparoscopic surgery in cancer

Laparoscopic surgery has been adopted across many cancer operations, with oncologic equivalence established by randomized trials.

*Colorectal cancer*. The COST trial (2004) randomized 872 colon cancer patients to laparoscopic versus open colectomy. The trial showed equivalent oncologic outcomes (recurrence, survival) with reduced morbidity in the laparoscopic group. Subsequent trials (CLASICC, COLOR, COLOR II) confirmed equivalence for colon cancer.

For rectal cancer, the situation is more complex. The ACOSOG Z6051 trial showed slightly inferior pathologic outcomes (margin clearance) with laparoscopic compared to open total mesorectal excision in low rectal cancer, though oncologic outcomes were similar at follow-up. The ALaCaRT trial showed similar results. Robotic rectal surgery (covered below) may provide better outcomes than straight laparoscopic for low rectal cancer.

*Gastric cancer*. Multiple Asian and Western trials have shown equivalent oncologic outcomes with laparoscopic versus open gastrectomy in selected patients (early gastric cancer, smaller tumors). The technique is increasingly standard at high-volume centers.

*Pancreatic surgery*. Minimally invasive distal pancreatectomy is well-established. Laparoscopic and robotic pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple procedure) is emerging at high-volume centers but technically demanding. The LEOPARD trial showed equivalent oncologic outcomes with reduced morbidity for distal pancreatectomy.

*Liver surgery*. Laparoscopic liver resection has been increasingly adopted, with international consensus statements (Louisville, Morioka) supporting its use for selected lesions. For colorectal liver metastases and hepatocellular carcinoma in non-cirrhotic livers, oncologic outcomes are equivalent.

*Gynecologic cancers*. Laparoscopic management of endometrial cancer (LAP2 trial) showed equivalent oncologic outcomes. However, the LACC trial (2018) showed inferior oncologic outcomes with laparoscopic radical hysterectomy for cervical cancer compared to open surgery. The reasons are unclear (possibly related to uterine manipulator use, CO2 effects on tumor cells, or other factors). Open surgery is now generally recommended for radical hysterectomy in cervical cancer.

*Urologic cancers*. Laparoscopic and (increasingly) robotic radical prostatectomy and nephrectomy are standard at most centers.

The pattern across these operations is that laparoscopic surgery is generally equivalent oncologically to open surgery for most cancers, with substantial benefits in recovery and morbidity. The exceptions (low rectal cancer, cervical cancer) are recognized and addressed through technique modifications or selection of patients for open surgery.

## Robotic surgery

Robotic-assisted surgery — currently dominated by the Intuitive Surgical da Vinci system — has become widespread. The technology provides:

*Three-dimensional visualization*. Stereoscopic cameras give the surgeon depth perception that straight laparoscopy lacks.

*Articulated instruments*. The robotic instruments have wristed joints that allow more dexterity than straight laparoscopic instruments. The surgeon's hand movements at the console are translated to instrument movements with scaling and tremor reduction.

*Ergonomic positioning*. The surgeon sits at a console rather than standing at the operating table. This reduces surgeon fatigue during long operations.

*Integration with imaging*. Robotic platforms increasingly integrate with intraoperative imaging (fluorescence imaging with indocyanine green, ultrasound, sometimes intraoperative MRI).

The applications of robotic surgery have expanded rapidly:

*Prostatectomy*. Robotic radical prostatectomy is now the dominant approach for prostate cancer in most US centers. The technique allows precise dissection in the narrow pelvic anatomy, which is particularly useful for nerve-sparing techniques. Functional outcomes (continence, erectile function) may be modestly better with robotic compared to open prostatectomy in experienced hands. Oncologic outcomes are equivalent.

*Hysterectomy and gynecologic oncology surgery*. Robotic hysterectomy is standard for early-stage endometrial cancer. As noted, robotic radical hysterectomy for cervical cancer has been called into question by the LACC trial.

*Colorectal surgery*. Robotic surgery for rectal cancer may provide better outcomes than straight laparoscopic for low pelvic dissection. The REAL trial and others have shown some advantages.

*Pancreatic surgery*. Robotic pancreaticoduodenectomy and distal pancreatectomy are increasingly performed at high-volume centers.

*Thoracic surgery*. Robotic lobectomy, esophagectomy, and other thoracic procedures.

*Head and neck cancer*. Transoral robotic surgery (TORS) for oropharyngeal cancers. The technique allows resection of base of tongue and tonsillar tumors through the mouth with the assistance of a robotic platform, avoiding the morbidity of traditional open neck dissection.

The cost of robotic surgery is substantial — the equipment is expensive, the instruments are single-use, and the surgical times can be longer (especially during the surgeon's learning curve). The cost-effectiveness varies by procedure and setting. For some operations (prostatectomy in particular), the functional benefits and patient recovery justify the cost. For others, the comparative cost-effectiveness is less clear.

Other surgical robot systems (Versius, Hugo, others) are entering the market, providing competition that may reduce costs over time.

## Image-guided and percutaneous interventions

Beyond surgery proper, *interventional radiology* and other image-guided approaches provide minimally invasive cancer treatments.

*Image-guided biopsy*. As discussed in Chapter 18A. Standard approach for most deep biopsies.

*Percutaneous ablation*. Destruction of localized tumors through needle-based approaches. Several modalities:
- *Radiofrequency ablation (RFA)*. Heat-based destruction via electrical current.
- *Microwave ablation*. Heat-based destruction via microwave energy.
- *Cryoablation*. Cold-based destruction.
- *Irreversible electroporation (IRE)*. Non-thermal destruction via electrical pulses.
- *High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU)*. Ultrasound-based heating without skin penetration.

Applications include hepatocellular carcinoma, renal cell carcinoma, lung metastases, bone metastases for palliation. The procedures are typically performed under image guidance with CT or ultrasound and require only a small skin puncture for needle insertion.

*Transarterial procedures*. Catheter-based delivery of treatment through the arterial system:
- *Transarterial chemoembolization (TACE)*. Delivery of chemotherapy plus embolic agents to the artery supplying a tumor. Standard for selected hepatocellular carcinoma.
- *Transarterial radioembolization (TARE)*. Delivery of radiation-emitting microspheres (yttrium-90) to a tumor's arterial supply.
- *Embolization for bleeding* tumors.

*Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT)*. Image-guided, high-precision radiation delivered in few fractions to small tumors. Covered in detail in Chapter 21B.

*Intraoperative imaging*. Real-time imaging during procedures to guide treatment. Examples include intraoperative ultrasound during liver surgery, intraoperative MRI during brain tumor resection.

These image-guided approaches are sometimes alternatives to surgery (focal ablation instead of resection for small renal masses or HCC) and sometimes complementary (image-guided biopsy before definitive treatment).

## Sentinel lymph node biopsy and lymphatic mapping

Sentinel lymph node biopsy was introduced in 20A. The technique is worth additional detail because it represents one of the major advances in cancer surgery.

*The technique*. A radiotracer (technetium-99m sulfur colloid) and/or blue dye is injected around the tumor (or in the periphery for breast cancer, in the dermis around the lesion for melanoma). The tracer migrates through lymphatic vessels to the first nodes that drain the tumor — the *sentinel nodes*. These nodes are identified intraoperatively with a gamma probe (for the radiotracer) and visualization (for the dye), removed, and examined pathologically.

*The biology*. The sentinel nodes are the first stop for any cancer cells that have spread to lymph nodes. If the sentinel nodes are clear of cancer, the probability that more distant nodes contain cancer is low. The technique avoids unnecessary lymphadenectomy in patients without nodal disease.

*Cancer applications*. SLNB is standard for:
- *Breast cancer*. Replaced routine ALND for clinically node-negative breast cancer.
- *Melanoma*. Standard for melanomas thick enough to warrant nodal assessment.
- *Vulvar cancer*. Replacing routine groin dissection.
- *Cervical cancer*. Emerging as a standard for early-stage disease.
- *Endometrial cancer*. Increasingly used in addition to or replacing full lymphadenectomy.
- *Penile cancer*. Standard approach.
- *Some head and neck cancers*. Emerging applications.

*Indocyanine green (ICG) fluorescence*. A newer addition to lymphatic mapping. ICG, a fluorescent dye, can be visualized with near-infrared imaging. It is increasingly used alone or in combination with traditional radiotracer for lymphatic mapping. The technique provides real-time visualization of lymphatic flow and may improve sentinel node identification rates.

The development of SLNB has been one of the major reductions in cancer surgery morbidity over the past 30 years. The morbidity of full lymphadenectomy (lymphedema, neurovascular injury, prolonged recovery) is now avoided for the majority of patients with clinically node-negative cancer.

## Reconstructive surgery

After resection of cancer, especially for cancers requiring substantial tissue removal, *reconstructive surgery* restores form and function. The reconstruction can occur immediately at the time of cancer resection or in delayed fashion later.

*Breast reconstruction*. After mastectomy or lumpectomy. Options include:
- *Implant-based reconstruction*. Tissue expanders placed at mastectomy, gradually expanded over months, then replaced with permanent implants. May be combined with acellular dermal matrix support.
- *Autologous tissue reconstruction*. Using the patient's own tissue from another body site. The deep inferior epigastric perforator (DIEP) flap uses abdominal tissue and skin without removing the underlying abdominal muscle. The transverse rectus abdominis muscle (TRAM) flap includes muscle. Other options include latissimus dorsi flap, gluteal flap, and others.
- *Combination approaches*. Sometimes implant plus autologous tissue for optimal results.

The decision between implant and autologous reconstruction involves trade-offs in cosmetic outcome, durability, complications, and recovery. Both can produce excellent results in experienced hands.

*Oncoplastic surgery*. Combining oncologic resection with plastic surgical techniques for the breast. The approach achieves wider resection margins while maintaining cosmetic outcomes. Volume displacement techniques rearrange breast tissue after lumpectomy; volume replacement techniques bring in tissue from outside the breast to fill the defect.

*Head and neck reconstruction*. After resection of oropharyngeal, oral cavity, or other head and neck cancers. Microvascular free flap reconstruction (radial forearm, fibular, anterolateral thigh, and others) restores form and function. The fibular free flap with skin and bone is particularly important for mandibular reconstruction. The technical complexity is substantial but functional outcomes have improved dramatically over decades.

*Sarcoma reconstruction*. After wide excision of soft tissue or bone sarcomas. May require flaps, bone grafts, limb-salvage techniques, or amputation. The development of limb-salvage surgery (preserving the limb rather than amputating) has been one of the major advances in sarcoma care over the past 50 years, made possible by neoadjuvant chemotherapy that shrinks tumors and reconstructive techniques that restore function.

*Gastrointestinal reconstruction*. After resection of esophagus, stomach, or intestine. Anastomosis to restore GI continuity. Specific techniques like the gastric pull-up after esophagectomy, neobladder formation after cystectomy, ileal pouch-anal anastomosis after proctocolectomy, and others.

*Genitourinary reconstruction*. Orthotopic neobladder, continent diversion, or incontinent ileal conduit after cystectomy. Urethral reconstruction. Phalloplasty or vaginoplasty after pelvic exenteration or genital cancer resection.

The integration of cancer surgery with reconstruction has been a major theme. The cancer surgeon, the plastic surgeon, and the patient jointly plan the resection and reconstruction. The advance of reconstructive techniques has allowed more aggressive cancer resection where needed (without leaving the patient with unacceptable defects) and has improved quality of life after cancer surgery.

## Functional preservation and quality of life

Beyond removing the cancer, modern surgical oncology emphasizes preserving function and quality of life. The principles:

*Nerve preservation*. In prostatectomy, preserving the cavernous nerves for erectile function (nerve-sparing technique). In thyroidectomy, preserving the recurrent laryngeal nerve. In rectal surgery, preserving the autonomic nerves for sexual and urinary function.

*Sphincter preservation*. In low rectal cancer, preserving the anal sphincter when possible (low anterior resection rather than abdominoperineal resection) to avoid permanent colostomy.

*Continence preservation*. In radical cystectomy with orthotopic neobladder. In rectal surgery with appropriate techniques.

*Lymph node preservation*. As discussed, SLNB minimizes lymphadenectomy and reduces lymphedema risk.

*Breast preservation*. Lumpectomy with radiation for many early-stage breast cancers, avoiding mastectomy when possible.

*Limb preservation*. Limb-salvage surgery for sarcomas.

*Cosmetic considerations*. Incision placement, oncoplastic techniques, reconstruction options all consider cosmetic outcomes.

The trade-off is always between adequate oncologic resection and preservation. Decisions are individualized — the same cancer in different patients may warrant different surgical approaches depending on patient priorities, anatomy, and surgical expertise.

*Patient-reported outcomes* are increasingly incorporated into surgical decision-making. Tools like the BREAST-Q for breast surgery patients, the EPIC-26 for prostate cancer patients, and other validated instruments allow systematic assessment of the patient's perspective on surgical outcomes. The data inform individual decisions and broader practice changes.

## Volume-outcome relationships

A major consideration in modern surgical oncology is the *volume-outcome relationship*. For complex cancer operations, surgical outcomes are better at high-volume centers performed by high-volume surgeons.

The relationship has been documented for many cancer operations:
- *Pancreaticoduodenectomy*. Mortality rates at high-volume centers (>20 cases/year) are 1-3 percent; at low-volume centers, 10-15 percent.
- *Esophagectomy*. Substantial volume-outcome differences in mortality and complications.
- *Cystectomy*. Volume affects mortality and oncologic outcomes.
- *Hepatectomy*. Major liver resections have substantial volume-outcome relationships.
- *Complex pelvic surgery*. Outcomes vary with center experience.

The Leapfrog Group, the National Quality Forum, and various other quality bodies recommend specific volume thresholds for complex cancer operations. Some health systems have implemented regionalization policies that direct complex cancer surgery to high-volume centers.

The volume-outcome relationship has implications:
- *Patient referral*. Patients undergoing complex cancer surgery should consider receiving care at high-volume centers when possible.
- *Surgeon training*. The learning curve for complex cancer operations is substantial; ongoing volume is needed to maintain skills.
- *Health system organization*. Concentrating cancer surgery at high-volume centers is associated with better outcomes; the trade-off is access (patients in rural or underserved areas face longer travel).
- *Telementoring and remote support*. Technology to extend high-volume expertise to lower-volume sites is being developed.

The pattern across surgical oncology is concentration of complex cases at specialized cancer centers, with general oncologic surgery distributed more broadly. The trend continues as procedures become more technically demanding.

## What this chapter gives you

Modern surgical oncology has refined cancer surgery to achieve equivalent or better oncologic outcomes with reduced patient burden. Minimally invasive approaches — laparoscopic, thoracoscopic, robotic surgery — are now standard for many cancer operations, with multiple randomized trials confirming oncologic equivalence and substantial recovery advantages.

The technologies vary by procedure. Laparoscopic surgery is established for many abdominal cancer operations (colorectal, gastric, gynecologic, urologic). Robotic surgery dominates prostatectomy and is increasingly used for rectal surgery, complex gynecologic surgery, and other operations. The cost of robotic surgery is substantial but justifies in selected indications.

Image-guided and percutaneous procedures (focal ablation, transarterial therapies, SBRT, image-guided biopsy) provide minimally invasive alternatives or complements to surgery for selected indications.

Sentinel lymph node biopsy has dramatically reduced the morbidity of nodal assessment for breast cancer, melanoma, and several other cancers. The technique is one of the major advances in cancer surgery over the past 30 years.

Reconstructive surgery — including breast reconstruction (implant or autologous), head and neck reconstruction with microvascular flaps, limb-salvage techniques for sarcomas, GI and GU reconstruction — restores form and function after necessary cancer resection. The integration of reconstructive options with oncologic planning has enabled more aggressive cancer treatment without unacceptable functional deficits.

Functional preservation — nerve-sparing, sphincter preservation, organ preservation, cosmetic considerations — is a major focus of modern surgical oncology. The balance between oncologic adequacy and quality of life is individualized.

Volume-outcome relationships in complex cancer surgery argue for concentration of difficult cases at high-volume centers, with implications for patient referral patterns and health system organization.

Chapter 21 turns to radiation oncology — the second major local treatment modality. The technologies (IMRT, IGRT, SBRT, proton therapy) and the biological principles underlying radiation therapy connect to the cancer biology covered throughout this book.
