Chemotherapy works because cancer cells divide faster than normal cells. Mostly.

Hold the qualifier. The original premise of cancer chemotherapy, dating to the discovery that nitrogen mustard could shrink lymphomas in the 1940s, was that drugs interfering with DNA synthesis or mitosis would preferentially kill rapidly dividing cells. The premise has produced one of the great medical achievements of the twentieth century — cancer drugs that cure or substantially extend life in many cancers, transforming what was uniformly fatal disease into something often treatable. The premise has also produced one of the great medical disappointments — drugs whose toxicity to normal proliferating tissues (bone marrow, gut, hair follicles, immune cells) creates substantial suffering, whose effectiveness in many cancers remains modest, and whose contribution to oncology has been progressively challenged by more targeted approaches.

The premise is also partly wrong. Many cancer cells don't actually divide faster than normal cells in the body. Hematopoietic stem cells divide more rapidly than many tumor cells. The proliferation differential between cancer and normal tissue is often modest. Chemotherapy works as well as it does in part because cancer cells have specific defects (in DNA repair, in apoptosis regulation, in metabolic flexibility) that make them more vulnerable to the damage chemotherapy causes, not just because they divide faster.

Despite the conceptual evolution, chemotherapy remains a backbone of modern oncology. It is used as primary curative therapy in some cancers (testicular cancer, many lymphomas, childhood acute lymphoblastic leukemia, gestational trophoblastic disease). It is used adjuvantly after surgery in many others. It is combined with targeted therapy, immunotherapy, and radiation to enhance their effects. It is used neoadjuvantly to shrink tumors before surgery. It is the standard of care for many metastatic cancers where targeted therapy options are limited.

This chapter — the first of two on chemotherapy — covers the principles of chemotherapy and the major drug classes. The next — 22B — covers pharmacology, resistance, toxicity, and supportive care. The chapter complements but does not replace the molecular details covered in earlier chapters (cell cycle in 9, apoptosis in 10, DNA repair in 4); it provides the clinical pharmacology that allows chemotherapy to be used effectively in patients.

Hold the question: *given that chemotherapy drugs work by damaging cells that are dividing or have biochemical vulnerabilities, how do we maximize tumor kill while minimizing normal tissue damage?*

## The historical context

Cancer chemotherapy began with a wartime accident. In 1943, an explosion in Bari harbor released nitrogen mustard, a chemical weapon, into the seawater. Soldiers exposed to the gas developed dramatic suppression of bone marrow and lymphoid tissue. The observation that nitrogen mustard depleted rapidly proliferating cells led to its evaluation as a cancer drug. By 1946, nitrogen mustard had been used successfully to treat lymphoma at Yale. The era of cancer chemotherapy had begun.

The 1950s and 60s saw rapid development of additional drugs. Folic acid antagonists (methotrexate) developed by Sidney Farber for childhood leukemia. 6-mercaptopurine developed by Gertrude Elion and George Hitchings. 5-fluorouracil developed by Charles Heidelberger for solid tumors. Vinca alkaloids isolated from periwinkle. Anthracyclines from soil bacteria. Cisplatin discovered serendipitously by Barnett Rosenberg in the 1960s.

The introduction of combination chemotherapy in the 1960s transformed treatment of leukemias and lymphomas. The MOPP regimen for Hodgkin lymphoma (mechlorethamine, vincristine, procarbazine, prednisone), developed by Vincent DeVita and colleagues, cured what had been uniformly fatal disease. The principles — using drugs with different mechanisms, different toxicity profiles, and different resistance patterns — became the template for cancer chemotherapy generally.

The 1970s-80s saw extension to solid tumors with mixed success. The combination chemotherapy approach cured most testicular cancers (cisplatin-based regimens), produced cures in some childhood cancers, and improved outcomes for breast and colorectal cancers when used adjuvantly. For many advanced solid tumors, however, chemotherapy extended life modestly without producing cures.

The 1990s-2000s saw the introduction of more chemotherapy agents (taxanes, gemcitabine, capecitabine, irinotecan, oxaliplatin, pemetrexed) and refinements of regimens. The 2000s onward have been dominated by targeted therapy and immunotherapy, but chemotherapy remains central, often in combination with these newer agents.

## The basic mechanisms of chemotherapy

Chemotherapy drugs damage cells through several mechanisms. The major categories:

*DNA damage*. Most chemotherapy drugs damage DNA either directly (binding to and modifying DNA) or indirectly (interfering with DNA synthesis). The damage triggers DNA damage response signaling, cell cycle arrest, and (if damage is severe) apoptosis or mitotic catastrophe.

*DNA synthesis inhibition*. Some drugs inhibit specific steps in DNA synthesis — folate metabolism (methotrexate), nucleotide synthesis (5-FU, gemcitabine), or polymerase activity (cytarabine, fludarabine).

*Mitotic interference*. Drugs that target the mitotic spindle (taxanes stabilize microtubules; vinca alkaloids prevent microtubule polymerization) prevent successful mitosis, leading to mitotic catastrophe.

*Topoisomerase inhibition*. Drugs that target topoisomerase I (irinotecan, topotecan) or topoisomerase II (etoposide, doxorubicin, mitoxantrone) stabilize DNA-enzyme complexes that produce DNA breaks.

*Specific molecular targeting*. Some chemotherapy drugs target specific molecular features. Asparaginase depletes asparagine, exploiting ALL cells' inability to synthesize this amino acid. Some agents (proteasome inhibitors, immunomodulators in multiple myeloma) work through mechanisms that bridge "chemotherapy" and "targeted therapy" categories.

The common theme is that chemotherapy drugs damage cells, and cells with adequate repair, intact apoptosis regulation, and metabolic flexibility can sometimes survive the damage. Cells with defects in these pathways — common in cancer — are preferentially killed.

The therapeutic window is narrow. The drugs that kill cancer cells also damage normal proliferating tissues. Modern chemotherapy practice involves careful dose selection, supportive care, and combination strategies to maximize cancer kill while keeping toxicity tolerable.

## Alkylating agents

Alkylating agents bind covalently to DNA, transferring alkyl groups (methyl, ethyl, or larger) to nucleophilic sites on DNA bases — most commonly the N7 position of guanine. The covalent binding produces multiple types of damage:
- *Monoadducts* on single bases.
- *Intrastrand crosslinks* between two bases on the same DNA strand.
- *Interstrand crosslinks* between bases on opposite strands of the DNA duplex.

Interstrand crosslinks are the most lethal lesion. They prevent strand separation needed for DNA replication and transcription.

Major alkylating agents:

*Cyclophosphamide*. A nitrogen mustard derivative. Activated by hepatic cytochrome P450 to phosphoramide mustard (the active metabolite) and acrolein (a toxic byproduct that causes hemorrhagic cystitis, mitigated by mesna in high-dose regimens). Used in breast cancer, lymphomas (CHOP, R-CHOP), some leukemias, and other cancers. Particularly important as a high-dose conditioning agent for stem cell transplant.

*Ifosfamide*. Related to cyclophosphamide. Used in sarcomas, lymphomas, and germ cell tumors. Causes hemorrhagic cystitis (requires mesna) and a characteristic encephalopathy syndrome.

*Melphalan*. A phenylalanine derivative. Used in multiple myeloma and as conditioning for some stem cell transplants.

*Chlorambucil*. An older alkylating agent. Used in CLL and lymphomas.

*Carmustine, lomustine* (nitrosoureas). Cross the blood-brain barrier. Used in brain tumors.

*Cisplatin, carboplatin, oxaliplatin* (platinum compounds). Form DNA crosslinks similar to alkylating agents (technically not alkylating agents but functionally similar mechanism). Cisplatin is one of the most important cancer drugs ever developed — cures most testicular cancers and is essential in many other regimens. Carboplatin has similar efficacy with less neuro- and ototoxicity. Oxaliplatin is essential in colorectal cancer regimens.

*Temozolomide*. An oral alkylating agent. Standard for glioblastoma in combination with radiation.

*Dacarbazine, procarbazine*. Methylating agents used in melanoma and lymphoma.

*Busulfan*. Used in CML (historical, before TKIs) and as conditioning for stem cell transplant.

Toxicities of alkylating agents include myelosuppression (uniform), nausea/vomiting (often severe), mucositis, alopecia, infertility (often permanent), and secondary malignancies (especially myelodysplasia and acute leukemia, with risk increasing with cumulative dose). Platinum compounds have specific toxicities (cisplatin: nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, ototoxicity; oxaliplatin: cumulative neuropathy).

## Antimetabolites

Antimetabolites resemble normal cellular metabolites (purines, pyrimidines, folates) and interfere with the biosynthesis of nucleotides or with DNA synthesis directly. They are S-phase-specific in action.

*Methotrexate*. A folate antagonist. Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, blocking the regeneration of tetrahydrofolate needed for thymidylate, purine, and amino acid synthesis. Used in many cancers (lymphomas, leukemias, osteosarcoma, breast cancer, head and neck cancer), in autoimmune diseases (rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis), and in ectopic pregnancy. High-dose methotrexate with leucovorin rescue is standard for some lymphomas. The drug requires careful pharmacokinetic monitoring at high doses.

*5-Fluorouracil (5-FU)*. A fluorinated pyrimidine. Inhibits thymidylate synthase and is incorporated into RNA, disrupting nucleotide metabolism and RNA processing. Backbone of colorectal cancer chemotherapy (FOLFOX, FOLFIRI regimens). Also used in gastric, pancreatic, breast, head and neck cancers, and others. Toxicities include diarrhea, mucositis, myelosuppression, hand-foot syndrome with prolonged infusion, and cardiac toxicity (rare but serious). The oral prodrug *capecitabine* converts to 5-FU in tissues and is increasingly used.

*Gemcitabine*. A cytidine analog. Inhibits DNA synthesis through multiple mechanisms (incorporation into DNA, inhibition of ribonucleotide reductase). Standard for pancreatic cancer; also used in bladder cancer, lung cancer, breast cancer, and others. Generally well-tolerated.

*Cytarabine (Ara-C)*. A cytidine analog. Incorporated into DNA, blocks elongation. Essential agent in acute myeloid leukemia (induction and consolidation). High-dose regimens are used for consolidation in younger AML patients.

*6-Mercaptopurine, 6-thioguanine*. Purine analogs. Used in childhood acute lymphoblastic leukemia, particularly during maintenance phase. Thiopurine S-methyltransferase (TPMT) genotype affects toxicity; testing is standard before initiation.

*Fludarabine*. An adenine analog. Used in CLL and lymphomas. Immunosuppressive (depletes T cells), increasing risk of opportunistic infections.

*Pentostatin, cladribine*. Adenosine deaminase inhibitors. Used in hairy cell leukemia (highly effective) and selected other diseases.

*Pemetrexed*. A multitargeted antifolate. Inhibits thymidylate synthase, dihydrofolate reductase, and other folate-dependent enzymes. Standard for non-squamous non-small-cell lung cancer and mesothelioma. Reduced toxicity with folic acid and vitamin B12 supplementation.

*Azacitidine, decitabine*. Cytidine analogs that inhibit DNA methyltransferase. Functionally hypomethylating agents (epigenetic therapy, Chapter 7B) rather than purely cytotoxic. Standard for myelodysplastic syndrome and elderly AML, often combined with venetoclax.

Antimetabolite toxicities include myelosuppression (variable depending on drug and dose), mucositis, diarrhea, hepatotoxicity, and drug-specific effects.

## Antitumor antibiotics

Antitumor antibiotics, despite the name, are not used for infections. They are natural products from microorganisms (mostly *Streptomyces* species) with potent antitumor activity. Most damage DNA through multiple mechanisms.

*Doxorubicin, daunorubicin, epirubicin, idarubicin* (anthracyclines). Intercalate into DNA, generate free radicals through redox cycling, and inhibit topoisomerase II. Among the most active and broadly used cancer drugs. Doxorubicin is used in lymphomas, breast cancer, sarcomas, AML (idarubicin/daunorubicin), and many other cancers. The major toxicity is *cumulative cardiotoxicity* — cardiomyopathy and heart failure related to total lifetime dose. Liposomal doxorubicin reduces cardiotoxicity. Dexrazoxane is used as a cardioprotectant. The cumulative dose limit (typically 450-550 mg/m² for doxorubicin) constrains lifetime exposure.

*Bleomycin*. Glycopeptide antibiotic. Generates reactive oxygen species that damage DNA. Used in Hodgkin lymphoma (ABVD regimen), germ cell tumors, and squamous cell cancers. Major toxicity is *pulmonary fibrosis* — typically dose-related but can occur unpredictably. Cumulative dose limits apply. Bleomycin lung toxicity is exacerbated by high inspired oxygen concentrations, which has practical implications for anesthesia management.

*Mitomycin C*. An alkylating agent (despite being classed with antibiotics historically). Used in bladder cancer (intravesical), some GI cancers, anal cancer (with 5-FU in chemoradiation).

*Actinomycin D (dactinomycin)*. Binds DNA and inhibits transcription. Used in pediatric sarcomas, gestational trophoblastic disease, and selected other diseases.

The antitumor antibiotics include some of the most potent chemotherapy drugs available. They are also associated with some of the most concerning long-term toxicities (cardiotoxicity, pulmonary fibrosis), requiring careful patient selection and monitoring.

## Mitotic inhibitors

Mitotic inhibitors target the microtubule cytoskeleton, preventing successful mitosis. Cells exposed to these drugs typically arrest in metaphase and eventually die through mitotic catastrophe or post-mitotic apoptosis.

*Vinca alkaloids* (vincristine, vinblastine, vinorelbine). Derived from periwinkle plants. Bind tubulin and prevent microtubule polymerization. The spindle cannot form properly; cells arrest in mitosis. Vincristine is essential in pediatric leukemias and lymphomas, with relatively low myelosuppression (the marrow-sparing feature is valuable in heavily pretreated patients). Vinblastine and vinorelbine have similar mechanisms with different toxicity profiles. Major toxicity is neuropathy (cumulative, dose-limiting). Vincristine has minimal myelosuppression; vinblastine and vinorelbine have more.

*Taxanes* (paclitaxel, docetaxel, nab-paclitaxel, cabazitaxel). Bind tubulin but unlike vinca alkaloids, they stabilize microtubules in their polymerized form. The spindle becomes too rigid to function; cells cannot progress through mitosis. Paclitaxel was originally derived from Pacific yew tree bark. Used in breast cancer, ovarian cancer, lung cancer, esophageal cancer, and many other cancers. Toxicities include neuropathy (cumulative), myelosuppression, hypersensitivity (requires premedication), and alopecia. Nab-paclitaxel (albumin-bound formulation) avoids the solvent that causes hypersensitivity with conventional paclitaxel. Cabazitaxel is used in castration-resistant prostate cancer.

*Eribulin*. A synthetic analog of halichondrin B, a marine natural product. Different microtubule-binding mechanism. Used in metastatic breast cancer.

*Ixabepilone*. An epothilone analog. Different microtubule-stabilizing mechanism than taxanes; sometimes effective in taxane-resistant disease.

The mitotic inhibitors illustrate how subtle differences in mechanism produce different clinical activity and resistance patterns. Taxane-resistant cancers may respond to vinca alkaloids or to eribulin; vincristine-resistant tumors may respond to taxanes. Cross-resistance is incomplete.

## Topoisomerase inhibitors

Topoisomerases manage DNA topology during replication and transcription. They make transient breaks in DNA, allow strand passage, and reseal the break. Drugs that inhibit topoisomerase stabilize the DNA-enzyme complex with the DNA broken, producing persistent DNA damage.

*Topoisomerase I inhibitors* (camptothecin derivatives).

*Irinotecan*. A camptothecin. Standard for colorectal cancer (FOLFIRI). Also used in pancreatic cancer (nano-liposomal formulation), gastric cancer, and other cancers. Toxicity is often diarrhea (acute cholinergic syndrome and delayed diarrhea), myelosuppression, alopecia. UGT1A1 genotype affects metabolism and toxicity; testing is sometimes done.

*Topotecan*. Used in small-cell lung cancer (second-line), ovarian cancer, and cervical cancer.

*Topoisomerase II inhibitors*.

*Etoposide*. Standard for testicular cancer, small-cell lung cancer, lymphomas, and other cancers. Particularly important as a high-dose agent in stem cell transplant conditioning. Major toxicity is myelosuppression. *Secondary leukemias* (typically AML with 11q23 translocations) occur in about 1-2 percent of patients receiving etoposide-containing regimens, sometimes years later.

*Anthracyclines* (doxorubicin, others). Already discussed under antitumor antibiotics; they also inhibit topoisomerase II as part of their mechanism.

*Mitoxantrone*. An anthracenedione related to anthracyclines. Less cardiotoxicity but still has cumulative cardiac effects.

The topoisomerase inhibitors have important roles in many cancer regimens. The risk of secondary leukemias with topoisomerase II inhibitors is a significant long-term consideration.

## Other agents

Several chemotherapy agents don't fit cleanly into the major categories:

*L-asparaginase* (and pegaspargase). Bacterial enzyme that depletes asparagine in plasma. ALL cells cannot synthesize asparagine adequately and require extracellular supply; asparaginase deprivation kills them. Cornerstone of pediatric ALL treatment for decades; increasingly used in adult ALL as well.

*Hydroxyurea*. Inhibits ribonucleotide reductase, blocking DNA synthesis. Used in CML (less common since TKIs), myeloproliferative neoplasms, sickle cell disease, and other contexts. Generally well-tolerated.

*Bortezomib, carfilzomib, ixazomib* (proteasome inhibitors). Inhibit the 26S proteasome, leading to accumulation of misfolded proteins and ER stress. Essential agents in multiple myeloma. Bortezomib causes neuropathy; carfilzomib has more cardiotoxicity.

*Lenalidomide, pomalidomide, thalidomide* (immunomodulatory drugs, IMiDs). Multiple mechanisms including direct cancer cell effects and immune modulation. Essential in multiple myeloma. Thalidomide is also used in selected other diseases. Teratogenic — requires careful management.

*Arsenic trioxide*. Active against acute promyelocytic leukemia. Combined with all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA), it produces high cure rates in APL without traditional chemotherapy.

*All-trans retinoic acid (ATRA)*. Differentiating agent in APL. Induces differentiation of the leukemic promyelocytes rather than killing them through cytotoxicity. Combined with arsenic trioxide or anthracycline, produces dramatic cure rates.

The "non-classical" agents reflect the recognition that cancer can be treated through many mechanisms beyond classical DNA damage and mitotic interference.

## Combination chemotherapy

Combination chemotherapy uses multiple drugs simultaneously or in sequence. The principles, articulated by Frei and others in the 1960s:

*Drugs with different mechanisms*. Each drug attacks the cancer through a different pathway, minimizing the possibility that a single mutation will produce resistance to all.

*Drugs with non-overlapping toxicities*. Each drug has different dose-limiting toxicities (myelosuppression for some, neuropathy for others, nephrotoxicity for still others). Combinations can deliver near-maximum doses of each drug without lethal cumulative toxicity.

*Drugs active as single agents*. Each drug in a combination should have demonstrated single-agent activity in the cancer being treated, ensuring that each contributes to efficacy.

*Optimal sequencing*. The order of drug administration matters in some combinations. Drugs that synergize biologically may work better given concurrently; drugs that compete may work better given sequentially.

The principles have produced curative regimens for many cancers:
- *Testicular cancer*. BEP (bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin) cures most metastatic disease.
- *Hodgkin lymphoma*. ABVD (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) or escalated BEACOPP for advanced disease.
- *Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma*. R-CHOP (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone).
- *Childhood ALL*. Multi-agent regimens (induction, consolidation, maintenance) over 2-3 years.
- *Breast cancer (adjuvant)*. Various regimens including anthracycline + taxane combinations.
- *Colorectal cancer*. FOLFOX (5-FU, leucovorin, oxaliplatin) or FOLFIRI (5-FU, leucovorin, irinotecan), often with biological agents.

The era of new chemotherapy regimen development has slowed since the 2000s. The combinations established in earlier decades remain backbones, while newer agents (targeted therapy, immunotherapy) are increasingly added to or substituted for parts of conventional regimens.

## Dose, schedule, and intensity

Cancer chemotherapy dosing follows several principles:

*Body surface area dosing*. Most drugs are dosed in mg/m² (milligrams per square meter of body surface area). The convention is historical and somewhat imprecise but pragmatically useful.

*Dose intensity*. The amount of drug given per unit time. Higher dose intensity (within tolerance) is associated with better outcomes in many cancers.

*Dose density*. The frequency of administration. Some regimens use dose-dense scheduling (drugs given every 2 weeks instead of every 3 weeks, with growth factor support to maintain marrow recovery). Dose-dense AC-T (adjuvant breast cancer) improved outcomes compared to conventional 3-week scheduling.

*Cumulative dose limits*. Some drugs have cumulative dose limits to prevent specific toxicities. Doxorubicin (cardiotoxicity), bleomycin (pulmonary fibrosis), vincristine (neuropathy), platinum agents (neuropathy and nephrotoxicity).

*Dose modifications*. Doses are reduced or held for toxicity. Common reasons: neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, mucositis, neuropathy, renal or hepatic dysfunction. The modifications affect dose intensity and may compromise outcome.

*Maximum tolerated dose (MTD) versus optimal biological dose (OBD)*. Conventional chemotherapy aims for MTD. Some newer agents (targeted therapy, immunotherapy) aim for OBD — the dose that achieves the biological effect, which may be below MTD.

*Therapeutic drug monitoring*. For some drugs, plasma levels are measured to optimize dosing. High-dose methotrexate (monitoring for prolonged elevation requiring leucovorin rescue). Busulfan in stem cell transplant. 5-FU (in some practices, monitoring to maintain steady-state level).

The art of chemotherapy involves balancing dose intensity with tolerability. Too much dose reduction compromises efficacy; too little compromises tolerance. Modern regimens are typically delivered at planned dose with proactive supportive care to maintain dose intensity.

## What this chapter gives you

Chemotherapy emerged from the observation in the 1940s that nitrogen mustard could deplete proliferating cells. The principle — drugs that damage DNA or interfere with mitosis preferentially kill cancer cells — has produced a backbone of cancer treatment with dramatic successes in some cancers and modest results in others.

The major chemotherapy classes are alkylating agents (DNA crosslinking), antimetabolites (interference with nucleotide synthesis or incorporation into DNA), antitumor antibiotics (DNA damage through multiple mechanisms), mitotic inhibitors (microtubule disruption), and topoisomerase inhibitors (stabilization of DNA-enzyme complexes producing breaks). Each class has multiple drugs with related mechanisms and overlapping but distinct toxicity profiles.

Combination chemotherapy regimens combine drugs with different mechanisms, non-overlapping toxicities, and demonstrated single-agent activity. Combinations have produced curative outcomes in many hematological malignancies, testicular cancer, and selected other diseases.

Specialty agents include asparaginase (ALL), proteasome inhibitors (multiple myeloma), immunomodulatory drugs (multiple myeloma), arsenic trioxide and ATRA (APL), and hypomethylating agents (MDS and elderly AML).

Dosing principles include body surface area calculation, dose intensity, dose density, cumulative dose limits, and therapeutic drug monitoring where applicable. The balance between adequate dose intensity and tolerable toxicity is one of the central considerations in chemotherapy practice.
