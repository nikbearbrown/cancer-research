# Chapter 28 — Cancer Screening: Finding It Early Enough to Cure


## TL;DR

- Screening is the second-best cancer intervention.
- The chapter moves through The principles of good screening, Cervical cancer screening, Breast cancer screening, Colorectal cancer screening, and related ideas.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

Screening is the second-best cancer intervention. The best is preventing the cancer entirely. The second best is catching it before it can kill you.

Hold the framing. Most cancers are curable if caught early enough. Stage I breast cancer has five-year survival above 99 percent. Stage I colorectal cancer has five-year survival around 90 percent. Stage I cervical cancer has five-year survival above 90 percent. The same cancers, once metastatic, kill most patients. The gap between stage I and stage IV survival is what screening tries to exploit — find the cancer at stage I, when it can be cured by local treatment, rather than at stage IV, when systemic therapy can only extend life.

But screening is not a clean win. Screening tests have false positives (people without cancer are told they may have cancer, get worked up, and suffer anxiety and procedure-related complications). Screening tests have false negatives (people with cancer are reassured incorrectly). Screening detects cancers that would never have killed the patient — overdiagnosis — and exposes those patients to unnecessary treatment. Screening can lead to surveillance bias (cancers detected earlier appear to have better prognosis simply because they were caught earlier, not because treatment was more effective). The mathematics of screening — Bayes' theorem applied to populations — is unforgiving, and good screening programs are harder to design than they look.

This chapter — the second of two on prevention and screening — covers the major screening programs in use, the principles that govern good versus bad screening, the controversies in the field, and the emerging technologies (liquid biopsy, multi-cancer detection) that may reshape how screening is done.

Hold the question: *given a screening test, when does its use save more lives than it disrupts?*

## The principles of good screening

Not every cancer is suitable for screening. Not every test, even one that detects cancer, is suitable for use in screening programs. The criteria for good screening were articulated by Wilson and Jungner in 1968 in a WHO report. They remain the framework against which screening programs are evaluated.

*The disease must be important.* High mortality, high morbidity, or both. A rare cancer with mild disease course doesn't warrant screening.

*The disease must have a detectable preclinical phase.* The cancer must be detectable before it becomes symptomatic, ideally for a meaningful period during which intervention is more effective than later treatment. Cancers that progress rapidly from initial transformation to clinical disease (like some aggressive lymphomas) don't benefit much from screening.

*Effective treatment must exist for early disease.* Detecting cancer early is only useful if early treatment changes outcomes. If the cancer kills regardless of stage, screening doesn't help. Some screening programs (PSA for prostate cancer) have been controversial partly because of debate about whether early detection translates to mortality benefit.

*The test must be acceptable to the screened population.* If the test is too invasive, painful, embarrassing, or expensive, uptake will be too low to produce population-level benefit. Colonoscopy faces this challenge — it works medically but compliance is limited.

*The test must perform well.* Adequate sensitivity (catches most cancers) and specificity (doesn't generate too many false positives). The positive predictive value (chance that a positive test indicates real cancer) depends on disease prevalence and test specificity.

*The benefits must outweigh the harms.* This is where most screening controversies live. Harms include false positives (anxiety, follow-up procedures), false negatives (delayed diagnosis), overdiagnosis (treating cancers that would never have caused harm), and radiation exposure or other test-specific risks.

*The cost must be reasonable.* Population-scale screening requires sustainable funding. The cost per life saved must be competitive with other health interventions.

A screening test that satisfies all these criteria is rare. Most accepted screening programs have at least one significant concern. The accepted programs are the ones where benefits clearly outweigh harms in well-defined populations.

## Cervical cancer screening

Cervical cancer screening is the most successful cancer screening program in history. The Pap smear — developed by Georgios Papanikolaou in the 1940s — detects abnormal cells from the cervix that may indicate precancerous lesions (cervical intraepithelial neoplasia, CIN) or invasive cancer.

The biology is what makes screening so effective. Cervical cancer arises from persistent HPV infection (Chapter 8B) through a slow progression: persistent infection → CIN 1 → CIN 2 → CIN 3 → carcinoma in situ → invasive cancer. The progression typically takes 10-20 years. Each step can be detected by cytology or HPV testing. Lesions at the CIN level can be removed by simple outpatient procedures (LEEP, cone biopsy). The cure is essentially guaranteed at this stage.

The result: in countries with widespread Pap smear screening, cervical cancer incidence dropped 50-70 percent after the screening became routine. In the US, cervical cancer mortality dropped from being a leading cause of cancer death in women in the 1940s to one of the less common causes today.

The screening has evolved. *HPV testing* — molecular detection of high-risk HPV types — is now used alone or in combination with cytology (co-testing). HPV testing is more sensitive than cytology for detecting high-grade lesions and is becoming the primary screening modality in many jurisdictions.

Current US Preventive Services Task Force recommendations:
- Ages 21-29: Pap test every 3 years.
- Ages 30-65: Pap test every 3 years, OR primary HPV testing every 5 years, OR co-testing every 5 years.
- Stop screening at 65 if prior screening has been adequate and negative.

The combination of HPV vaccination (Chapter 17A) and ongoing screening is reshaping cervical cancer epidemiology. The vaccine prevents the infection that drives the cancer; screening catches the precancers that develop in unvaccinated women. Australia's combined program is on track to effectively eliminate cervical cancer.

The global situation is uneven. In low- and middle-income countries with limited screening infrastructure, cervical cancer remains a leading cause of female cancer death. Visual inspection with acetic acid (VIA), HPV self-testing, and other simplified approaches are being implemented to extend screening to under-served populations.

## Breast cancer screening

Mammography is the most widely used cancer screening test, but also one of the most contested.

The technology: X-ray imaging of breast tissue, with newer digital and tomosynthesis (3D) versions providing better resolution. Mammography can detect breast tumors smaller than can be felt, sometimes by years.

The clinical trials of mammography screening — conducted from the 1960s through the 1990s — generally showed mortality benefit. The HIP trial (1963-1969), the Two-County trial (1977-1985), the Edinburgh trial, and others demonstrated 20-30 percent reductions in breast cancer mortality with regular mammography in women 50-69. The mortality benefit in women 40-49 is more modest and contested.

The current US Preventive Services Task Force recommendations (updated 2024):
- Ages 40-74: Mammography every 2 years.
- Ages 75+: Insufficient evidence to recommend for or against.

The European recommendations have generally favored starting at 50 rather than 40, reflecting different views of the benefit-harm calculation in younger women.

The controversies are several:

*Overdiagnosis*. Mammography detects ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) and small invasive cancers that may not have progressed to clinical disease. Estimates of overdiagnosis range from 5 percent to 30 percent of screen-detected cancers, with much debate about the precise number. Women diagnosed with screen-detected cancer cannot know whether their cancer would have killed them; the population-level numbers are what matter.

*False positives*. Mammograms have meaningful false positive rates. Over 10 years of annual screening, about 60 percent of women will have at least one false-positive result requiring additional workup. The anxiety and the procedures (biopsies, additional imaging) are real harms.

*Radiation exposure*. Mammography delivers a small radiation dose (about 0.4 mSv per exam). The lifetime cancer risk from cumulative screening radiation is small but not zero.

*Density and screening accuracy*. Dense breast tissue reduces mammography sensitivity. Women with dense breasts may benefit from supplemental screening (ultrasound, MRI) but the optimal protocol is debated.

The mammography debate has been intense and remains active. The fundamental tension is between population-level mortality benefit (modest but real) and individual-level harms (significant for some women). Reasonable people disagree about how to weigh these.

*MRI screening* for high-risk women (BRCA1/2 carriers, women with prior chest radiation, women with strong family history) provides higher sensitivity than mammography and is recommended in addition to mammography for women with elevated risk.

## Colorectal cancer screening

Colorectal cancer is well-suited for screening because of its slow progression through detectable precancerous adenomatous polyps. The adenoma-to-carcinoma sequence (Chapter 4) takes 10-15 years on average, with polyps detectable for most of that time.

Several screening tests are available:

*Colonoscopy*. The most sensitive and specific test. A flexible scope passes through the entire colon, allowing direct visualization, biopsy, and polyp removal in the same procedure. Cancer prevention is direct — removing precancerous polyps prevents the cancer they would have become. The major disadvantages are invasiveness, the need for bowel preparation, sedation requirements, and cost.

*Flexible sigmoidoscopy*. Examines only the rectum and lower colon. Less sensitive than colonoscopy but also less invasive and less expensive. Combined with stool tests, it provides a reasonable screening option.

*Fecal occult blood test (FOBT)* and *fecal immunochemical test (FIT)*. Detect blood in stool. FIT is more sensitive than older FOBT. Annual testing with follow-up colonoscopy for positive results is an alternative to direct colonoscopy.

*Stool DNA tests* (Cologuard) detect cancer-related DNA changes in stool. Lower sensitivity than colonoscopy for adenomas but higher than FIT alone. Used at 3-year intervals.

*CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy)*. CT-based imaging of the colon. Less invasive than optical colonoscopy but requires bowel preparation and still requires follow-up colonoscopy for positive findings.

Current US Preventive Services Task Force recommendations:
- Adults 45-75: Screening with one of: colonoscopy every 10 years, sigmoidoscopy every 5 years (with FIT every year), CT colonography every 5 years, FIT every year, FIT-DNA every 3 years, or stool guaiac every year. (The choice depends on patient preference, access, and risk factors.)
- Ages 76-85: Individualized decision-making.
- Past 85: Generally not recommended.

The screening age recommendation was lowered from 50 to 45 in 2021 because of rising colorectal cancer rates in younger adults, particularly affecting people in their 30s and 40s. The reasons for this rise (the "early-onset colorectal cancer" trend) are not fully understood but may relate to changes in diet, microbiome, obesity, and other factors.

Colonoscopy screening — when uptake is good — substantially reduces colorectal cancer mortality. The mortality reduction from screening alone is estimated at 30-50 percent in high-uptake settings. Combined with rising HPV vaccination and improving treatment, this could be one of the cleaner cancer outcomes stories of the next decade.

## Lung cancer screening

Lung cancer screening with low-dose computed tomography (LDCT) was established as effective in the National Lung Screening Trial (NLST), published in 2011. The trial randomized 53,000 heavy smokers and former heavy smokers to either LDCT or chest X-ray screening; LDCT reduced lung cancer mortality by 20 percent.

Current US Preventive Services Task Force recommendations (updated 2021):
- Ages 50-80 with at least 20 pack-years smoking history, currently smoking or quit within the past 15 years: Annual LDCT.

The recommendation was expanded from the original 2013 recommendation (55-80, 30 pack-years) to extend the benefit to more people who would benefit. Coverage by Medicare and most commercial insurers is now standard.

The benefits are clear in well-selected populations. The harms are real:
- *False positives*. About 25-30 percent of LDCT scans show nodules that require further workup. Most are benign.
- *Overdiagnosis*. Some screen-detected lung cancers (especially indolent slow-growing adenocarcinomas) might never have caused symptoms.
- *Radiation exposure*. LDCT delivers about 1.5 mSv per scan, less than diagnostic CT but more than mammography.
- *Procedural complications*. Biopsies of suspicious nodules carry risks (pneumothorax, bleeding, infection).

Uptake of lung cancer screening has been disappointing — less than 10 percent of eligible patients are screened in the US, compared to over 70 percent screened for colorectal cancer in eligible adults. The reasons include lack of awareness, clinician inertia, smoking stigma (patients feeling responsible for their disease and not seeking care), and access issues.

Active smoking interventions (cessation counseling) at the time of screening are essential. Lung cancer screening without cessation support misses an opportunity to prevent the disease in active smokers.

## Prostate cancer screening

Prostate cancer screening is the most controversial cancer screening program. The principal screening test, prostate-specific antigen (PSA), detects prostate cancer but also detects clinically irrelevant disease that would never have killed the patient. The result is substantial overdiagnosis and overtreatment.

The clinical trials of PSA screening produced inconsistent results. The European Randomized Study of Screening for Prostate Cancer (ERSPC) showed about a 20 percent reduction in prostate cancer mortality after 13 years of follow-up; the US PLCO trial showed no benefit. The interpretation of these conflicting results has driven the screening debate.

The current US Preventive Services Task Force recommendations:
- Ages 55-69: Individualized decision after discussing the modest potential benefit and substantial harms.
- Ages 70+: Not recommended.

The "individualized decision" is meaningful — the recommendation acknowledges that some men will benefit from screening (catching aggressive cancers early) while others will be harmed (diagnosed with indolent cancer they would have died with rather than of, then treated with surgery or radiation that causes erectile dysfunction and incontinence).

The screening landscape is evolving. The current approach involves:
- Initial PSA testing in selected men with informed consent about risks.
- For elevated PSA, additional risk stratification using PSA density, free-to-total PSA ratio, PSA velocity, MRI of the prostate, prostate cancer biomarker panels.
- Active surveillance for low-risk disease, with intervention only when there is evidence of progression.

The active surveillance approach has been a major shift. Men with Gleason 3+3=6 prostate cancer (the most common pattern of screen-detected disease) are now often managed with surveillance rather than immediate treatment. The cancer is detected, but the patient is spared the harms of unnecessary treatment.

The continued controversy reflects the fundamental challenge: PSA is a sensitive but non-specific test that detects many indolent cancers. Distinguishing the cancers that will kill from those that won't is the key, and current biomarker technology is gradually improving the discrimination.

## Other screening contexts

*Skin cancer*. No formal screening program is recommended for the general population, but self-examination and dermatologic surveillance are encouraged for high-risk individuals (fair skin, multiple atypical nevi, family history of melanoma, prior melanoma, immunosuppression). Total body photography and dermoscopy enhance detection of suspicious lesions.

*Liver cancer*. Hepatocellular carcinoma screening with alpha-fetoprotein and abdominal ultrasound is recommended for patients with cirrhosis (any cause) every 6 months. Screening in non-cirrhotic populations is not recommended due to low incidence.

*Cervical cancer screening for transgender people*. Considerations vary based on gender-affirming care, with cervical screening recommended for individuals with retained cervical tissue regardless of gender identity. The framework requires individualized approach.

*Familial cancer syndromes*. Intensive surveillance for high-risk hereditary cancer syndromes (BRCA1/2, Lynch syndrome, Li-Fraumeni, etc.) is a specialized form of screening. The protocols often start earlier than population screening and use more sensitive technologies (MRI, frequent endoscopy).

*Pancreatic cancer*. Currently no recommended screening for the general population. High-risk individuals (BRCA2 carriers, familial pancreatic cancer kindreds, Peutz-Jeghers syndrome) may benefit from endoscopic ultrasound or MRI surveillance.

*Ovarian cancer*. Screening with CA-125 and transvaginal ultrasound has not shown mortality benefit and is not recommended for general population. High-risk women (BRCA carriers) are managed individually, often with prophylactic salpingo-oophorectomy rather than screening.

## Emerging screening: multi-cancer early detection

A major frontier in cancer screening is *multi-cancer early detection (MCED)* — blood tests that can detect multiple cancers simultaneously, often before symptoms.

The leading commercial test is *Galleri* (GRAIL), a methylation-based ctDNA test that screens for 50+ cancers from a single blood sample. The test detects cancer signals in ctDNA and uses machine learning to predict the tissue of origin. Sensitivity varies by cancer type and stage — higher in advanced stages and in cancers with high ctDNA shed, lower in early-stage cancers and low-shedding cancers.

Other multi-cancer screening approaches: cell-free DNA fragmentation analysis (DELFI), protein biomarker panels (CancerSEEK), and integrated approaches combining multiple signals.

The promise is real. A blood-based test that could screen for many cancers simultaneously would address the access and compliance problems of single-cancer screening. Early detection could be applied broadly across populations rather than only to those eligible for specific screening programs.

The challenges are substantial:
- *Sensitivity at early stages*. Stage I cancers shed limited ctDNA. Detection at the curable stage where screening adds most value is the hardest.
- *Specificity and false positives*. Population screening of 50+ cancers will produce false positives. A 99 percent specific test screening 1000 people will produce ~10 false positives, each requiring expensive workup.
- *Tissue of origin*. When the test signals "cancer present," it must also indicate where the cancer is. Current TOO accuracy is improving but imperfect.
- *Clinical utility*. Even with accurate detection, demonstrating that screening-detected cancers have better outcomes than those detected by symptoms requires large prospective trials. These are underway but will take years to complete.
- *Cost*. MCED tests are expensive (currently $949 for Galleri, not insurance-covered for most uses).

The NCI is conducting large prospective trials (the PATHFINDER study series and others) to evaluate MCED clinical utility. The UK NHS is conducting NHS-Galleri, a 140,000-person study of Galleri in the UK population. Results from these trials over the next 5-10 years will determine whether MCED becomes a standard screening modality.

## Lead-time bias, length bias, and overdiagnosis

A discussion of screening would be incomplete without the statistical artifacts that confound interpretation of screening outcomes.

*Lead-time bias*. Earlier detection by screening necessarily increases the time from diagnosis to death, even if treatment is no better. A cancer detected by screening at age 50 with death at age 60 appears to have 10 years of survival; the same cancer detected by symptoms at age 55 with death at age 60 has 5 years of survival. Both patients died at the same age, but the screening case appears to have had longer survival. The illusion is corrected by looking at age at death rather than time from diagnosis to death.

*Length bias*. Screening preferentially detects slow-growing cancers (which spend longer in the detectable preclinical phase). Fast-growing aggressive cancers are more likely to be detected by symptoms in the intervals between screening tests. This selection means screen-detected cancers, on average, are inherently more indolent than symptom-detected ones — they would have done better even without earlier detection.

*Overdiagnosis*. The most consequential bias. Screening can detect cancers that would never have caused symptoms in the patient's lifetime. The patient is told they have cancer, undergoes treatment with its associated harms, but never would have had the disease without screening. The phenomenon is particularly severe in:
- Prostate cancer screening (PSA-detected indolent disease in older men).
- Thyroid cancer screening (papillary microcarcinomas common at autopsy in unscreened populations).
- Small lung nodule detection on LDCT.
- Some breast cancer detection (especially DCIS and slow-growing invasive cancers in older women).

Estimates of overdiagnosis vary by cancer and screening intensity. Even modest overdiagnosis rates produce substantial cumulative impact when applied at population scale.

The corrective is to evaluate screening by population-level cancer mortality reduction, ideally in randomized trials with long-term follow-up. The disease-specific mortality rate at long-term follow-up captures the real benefit of screening, separated from the biases that complicate diagnosis-based metrics.

## What this chapter gives you

Cancer screening can detect disease early enough to cure it. The most successful programs — cervical, colorectal, mammography — have reduced disease-specific mortality substantially. But screening is not a clean win. False positives, false negatives, overdiagnosis, and screening biases complicate the benefit-harm calculation.

The screening landscape currently includes well-established programs (cervical, breast, colorectal), more controversial programs (lung, prostate), and emerging programs (multi-cancer early detection). Each has its own evidence base, its own benefit-harm profile, and its own population for which it makes sense.

The principles articulated by Wilson and Jungner in 1968 remain the framework for evaluating screening: important disease, detectable preclinical phase, effective early treatment, acceptable test, good test performance, favorable benefit-harm ratio, reasonable cost. Programs that meet all the criteria are rare; programs that meet most are the established ones.

The future of screening likely involves *risk-stratified approaches* that target high-risk populations more intensively while reducing screening intensity in low-risk populations. Polygenic risk scores, family history integration, lifestyle factors, and emerging biomarkers can identify who would benefit most from screening. Multi-cancer detection approaches promise to broaden screening to more cancers and more patients but require robust clinical validation.

The integration of primary prevention (17A) with secondary prevention (this chapter) is the conceptual framework for population-level cancer control. Tobacco control, HPV vaccination, and other primary prevention reduce the cancers that occur. Screening catches early the cancers that do occur. Tertiary prevention (the rest of this book) treats the cancers that progress despite prevention and screening. Each layer contributes to the overall reduction in cancer mortality.

Chapter 18 turns to cancer detection and diagnosis once a tumor is suspected — the imaging, biopsy, pathology, and molecular workup that establishes the diagnosis and guides treatment.
