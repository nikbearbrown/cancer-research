# Chapter 38 — Chemotherapy: Pharmacology, Resistance, and Toxicity Management


## TL;DR

- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

A drug that kills cancer cells in a dish is one thing. A drug that kills cancer cells in a patient without killing the patient is another thing entirely.

Hold the framing. The journey from preclinical activity to clinical utility for any chemotherapy drug runs through a pharmacological gauntlet. The drug must be absorbed (or injectable) reliably. It must reach the tumor at therapeutic concentrations. It must be metabolized predictably enough that dosing is feasible. It must be excreted without producing toxicity in eliminating organs. It must spare normal tissues enough that the patient survives the treatment. Drugs that fail any of these tests don't make it from the laboratory to the clinic, regardless of their preclinical activity.

Even drugs that pass these tests face the challenge of *drug resistance*. Cancer cells exposed to chemotherapy survive in proportion to their pre-existing or acquired resistance mechanisms. Resistance emerges in essentially every patient with metastatic cancer on long-term chemotherapy. Understanding the resistance mechanisms — and how to circumvent them — is one of the central challenges of oncology.

And even when chemotherapy works, the *toxicities* limit what we can deliver. Bone marrow suppression. Mucositis. Nausea. Neuropathy. Cardiotoxicity. Each toxicity has specific management strategies that have evolved over decades. Modern supportive care has made chemotherapy substantially more tolerable than it was 30 years ago, but the toxicities remain real and often dose-limiting.

This chapter — the second on chemotherapy — covers pharmacology and pharmacokinetics, the mechanisms of resistance, the major toxicities and their management, and the supportive care infrastructure that makes chemotherapy delivery possible. The chapter complements 22A, which covered the principles and drug classes.

Hold the question: *given the drugs we have, how do we deliver them to maximize benefit and minimize harm?*

## Pharmacology and pharmacokinetics

Cancer chemotherapy drugs follow standard pharmacological principles, with specific considerations.

*Absorption*. Most chemotherapy is administered intravenously, bypassing absorption issues. Some agents are oral (capecitabine, temozolomide, hydroxyurea, oral methotrexate, oral cyclophosphamide, oral etoposide). Oral chemotherapy has the convenience advantage but introduces variability in absorption and adherence.

*Distribution*. Drug distribution depends on physicochemical properties (lipid solubility, protein binding, ionization at physiological pH) and physiological factors (blood flow to tissues, vascular permeability, tissue barriers like the blood-brain barrier). Most chemotherapy drugs reach most tissues to some extent, but some are heavily protein-bound (limiting free drug availability) and some are excluded from sanctuary sites like the CNS.

*Metabolism*. Many chemotherapy drugs are metabolized by hepatic enzymes, primarily cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP2D6, CYP3A4, others). Some require metabolic activation to active forms (cyclophosphamide → phosphoramide mustard via CYP enzymes; 5-FU activation; capecitabine → 5-FU; irinotecan → SN-38). Others are inactivated by metabolism. Drug-drug interactions through CYP induction or inhibition can substantially affect chemotherapy levels and toxicity.

*Excretion*. Most chemotherapy drugs are eliminated by hepatic metabolism with biliary excretion, by renal excretion, or both. Specific agents have specific elimination pathways:
- Cisplatin: primarily renal — dose reduction needed in renal impairment.
- Methotrexate: renal — careful dose adjustment for renal function; high-dose regimens require hydration and urinary alkalinization.
- Capecitabine, 5-FU: extensively metabolized, with dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase (DPD) as a key enzyme.
- Doxorubicin: hepatic metabolism — dose adjustment for liver dysfunction.

The pharmacokinetic principles affect dosing. Renal impairment, hepatic dysfunction, age, body composition, and concomitant medications all influence appropriate dosing for specific drugs.

*Pharmacogenomics*. Genetic variation in drug-metabolizing enzymes affects chemotherapy toxicity and efficacy. Specific examples:
- *TPMT and 6-MP/6-TG*. Thiopurine S-methyltransferase polymorphisms predict severe toxicity from thiopurines. TPMT genotype or activity is increasingly tested before initiating these drugs.
- *UGT1A1 and irinotecan*. UGT1A1 polymorphisms (especially UGT1A1*28) predict severe diarrhea from irinotecan. Testing can guide dosing.
- *DPYD and 5-FU/capecitabine*. Dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase variants predict severe toxicity from fluoropyrimidines. Pre-treatment testing is recommended in some jurisdictions (mandatory in Europe).
- *CYP2D6 and tamoxifen*. CYP2D6 variants affect conversion to active endoxifen; clinical relevance has been debated.

Pharmacogenomic testing is increasingly integrated into chemotherapy practice, though implementation is uneven.

## Drug resistance mechanisms

Cancer cell resistance to chemotherapy emerges through several mechanisms. The resistance can be *intrinsic* (present before treatment, allowing the cancer to be resistant from the start) or *acquired* (developing during or after treatment).

*Reduced drug uptake*. Some drugs require specific transporters to enter cells. Methotrexate enters cells through reduced folate carrier (RFC); cells with reduced RFC expression are methotrexate-resistant. Cisplatin uptake depends in part on the copper transporter CTR1.

*Increased drug efflux*. ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters pump drugs out of cells. P-glycoprotein (ABCB1/MDR1) effluxes many chemotherapy drugs (taxanes, vinca alkaloids, anthracyclines, etoposide). Multidrug resistance protein 1 (MRP1/ABCC1) and breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP/ABCG2) also efflux multiple drugs. Cancer cells with elevated ABC transporter expression are multi-drug resistant. P-glycoprotein inhibitors were extensively tested in the 1990s and 2000s but failed to improve outcomes in clinical trials, partly due to off-target effects of the inhibitors and the multiple parallel efflux mechanisms.

*Drug inactivation*. Cancer cells can metabolize drugs to inactive forms. Glutathione conjugation by glutathione S-transferases inactivates alkylating agents and platinum compounds. Aldehyde dehydrogenases inactivate some chemotherapy intermediates (cyclophosphamide derivatives). Cytidine deaminase inactivates cytidine analogs.

*Alterations in drug targets*. Mutations or altered expression of drug target proteins:
- Reduced thymidylate synthase mutations → 5-FU resistance.
- Altered topoisomerase II → anthracycline and etoposide resistance.
- Tubulin mutations → vinca alkaloid and taxane resistance (rare).
- Altered DNA repair enzymes → variable effects.

*Enhanced DNA damage repair*. Cells with increased DNA repair capacity survive DNA-damaging chemotherapy. Specific examples:
- *MGMT* expression → resistance to alkylating agents (especially temozolomide in glioblastoma).
- *ERCC1* expression → cisplatin resistance.
- Enhanced homologous recombination → cisplatin and PARP inhibitor resistance (the latter especially through BRCA reversion mutations).
- BCL-2 family overexpression → resistance to various drugs that trigger apoptosis.

*Apoptosis evasion*. Defects in apoptotic pathways allow cells to survive damage that should trigger cell death:
- *TP53* mutations are associated with resistance to chemotherapy across many drug classes.
- BCL-2 family alterations (BCL-2 overexpression, BAX loss) confer resistance.
- Activation of survival signaling (PI3K-AKT, NF-κB) provides anti-apoptotic signaling.

*Cell cycle alterations*. Most chemotherapy targets dividing cells. Cells in quiescence (G0) are protected. Cancer stem cells (Chapter 15A) often have quiescent characteristics that contribute to resistance.

*Microenvironment-mediated resistance*. The tumor microenvironment can protect cancer cells:
- Dense matrix impedes drug penetration.
- Hypoxia reduces effectiveness of some drugs (especially those requiring oxygen for action, like certain radiation sensitizers and some chemotherapy through reactive oxygen species).
- Stromal cells secrete protective factors.

*Multidrug resistance*. Resistance can affect multiple unrelated drugs simultaneously. The classical mechanism is P-glycoprotein overexpression. Other mechanisms (anti-apoptotic protein upregulation, enhanced DNA repair, microenvironment effects) can also produce multi-drug resistance.

The clinical implication is that resistance is often the limiting factor in treatment response. First-line chemotherapy may work; second-line is less effective; third-line is often less effective still. Combination approaches and rotation of drugs aim to delay or overcome resistance.

## Common toxicities and their management

Chemotherapy toxicities span many organ systems. Modern supportive care has dramatically improved the management of many of these.

*Myelosuppression*. Suppression of bone marrow function affects neutrophils (neutropenia), platelets (thrombocytopenia), and red cells (anemia). Each has specific implications:

- *Neutropenia* increases infection risk dramatically. *Febrile neutropenia* (fever in a neutropenic patient) is a medical emergency requiring prompt antibiotic therapy. The nadir typically occurs 7-14 days after most chemotherapy. *Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF, filgrastim, pegfilgrastim)* can be used prophylactically (typically when febrile neutropenia risk exceeds 20% with the chosen regimen) or therapeutically. The drugs accelerate neutrophil recovery and reduce febrile neutropenia incidence.

- *Thrombocytopenia* increases bleeding risk. Platelet transfusions are given for clinically significant thrombocytopenia (typically below 10,000-20,000 per μL for prophylactic transfusion; higher thresholds for active bleeding or procedures). *Thrombopoietin receptor agonists (romiplostim, eltrombopag, avatrombopag)* are increasingly used to support platelet recovery, though their role in chemotherapy-induced thrombocytopenia is still being defined.

- *Anemia*. Can cause fatigue and impair function. Transfusion is given for significant symptoms or low hemoglobin (typically below 7-8 g/dL). *Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs)* (epoetin, darbepoetin) raise hemoglobin but have been associated with worse cancer outcomes in some studies; their use is now restricted to specific contexts with careful counseling.

*Nausea and vomiting*. Once a uniform feature of chemotherapy and a major reason patients refused treatment, now substantially controllable with modern antiemetics. The risk depends on the chemotherapy agent (cisplatin, anthracyclines, cyclophosphamide are high-risk; others lower risk). The standard antiemetic approach for highly emetogenic chemotherapy involves a combination of:
- *5-HT3 receptor antagonists* (ondansetron, granisetron, palonosetron).
- *NK1 receptor antagonists* (aprepitant, fosaprepitant, netupitant, rolapitant).
- *Corticosteroids* (dexamethasone).
- *Olanzapine* (an antipsychotic with antiemetic activity, increasingly used).

The combination has reduced acute nausea/vomiting from a major problem to a manageable concern in most patients.

*Mucositis*. Inflammation of mucosal surfaces — most commonly oral, but can affect the entire GI tract. Causes pain, difficulty eating, and risk of infection. Management includes:
- Oral hygiene with bland rinses.
- Topical analgesics (lidocaine rinses).
- Systemic pain medication for severe cases.
- Nutritional support, sometimes feeding tubes for severe disease.
- *Palifermin* (keratinocyte growth factor) — used for severe oral mucositis in hematopoietic stem cell transplant settings.
- Cryotherapy (oral ice) during 5-FU infusion — reduces oral mucositis incidence.

*Alopecia*. Hair loss is psychologically significant for many patients. Most chemotherapy causes some degree of alopecia; some agents cause complete hair loss while others cause partial. *Scalp cooling* (cold caps applied during chemotherapy infusion) can reduce alopecia incidence for some regimens, particularly in breast cancer.

*Fatigue*. The most common chemotherapy symptom, often persisting after treatment. Management involves treating reversible causes (anemia, sleep disturbance, depression, thyroid dysfunction), encouraging physical activity (a counterintuitive but effective intervention), and addressing pain and other symptoms that worsen fatigue.

*Diarrhea*. Common with several drugs (5-FU, irinotecan, capecitabine). Severe diarrhea can produce dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, and septic complications from intestinal microbial translocation. Management with hydration, antimotility agents (loperamide; octreotide for severe cases), and dose reduction.

*Peripheral neuropathy*. Caused by platinum agents (especially cisplatin and oxaliplatin), taxanes, vinca alkaloids, bortezomib, and others. Often dose-cumulative. Can be sensory (numbness, tingling, pain) or motor (weakness). Acute oxaliplatin neuropathy is triggered or worsened by cold exposure. Once established, treatment options are limited (duloxetine has some evidence for chronic chemotherapy-induced neuropathy). Prevention through dose modification when neuropathy develops is the main strategy.

*Cardiotoxicity*. Specific drugs (anthracyclines, trastuzumab, some targeted therapies) cause cardiac dysfunction. Cumulative dose limits, echocardiographic monitoring, and cardioprotective strategies (dexrazoxane for anthracyclines, ACE inhibitors and beta-blockers for established dysfunction) are standard.

*Hepatotoxicity*. Variable across drugs. Monitoring liver function and dose modification for elevations.

*Nephrotoxicity*. Cisplatin is the major culprit. Hydration, magnesium replacement, and dose adjustment are standard. Carboplatin has less nephrotoxicity.

*Pulmonary toxicity*. Bleomycin (fibrosis), some other agents. Monitoring pulmonary function and limiting cumulative dose.

*Secondary malignancies*. Long-term concern for several chemotherapy regimens. Alkylating agents and topoisomerase II inhibitors are particular concerns. The risk is typically 1-5% over a decade or longer. Most concerning for childhood cancer survivors and young adults with cured cancers, where the long expected lifespan creates substantial cumulative risk.

*Infertility*. Many chemotherapy regimens cause infertility. Pre-treatment fertility preservation (sperm banking, oocyte cryopreservation, embryo cryopreservation, ovarian tissue cryopreservation) should be discussed with patients of reproductive age before initiating gonadotoxic treatment.

*Cognitive impairment ("chemo brain")*. Many cancer survivors report persistent cognitive symptoms after chemotherapy. The phenomenon is real and includes effects on memory, attention, and processing speed. The mechanism is incompletely understood; multiple factors likely contribute (direct chemotherapy effects, inflammation, treatment-related menopause, fatigue, sleep disturbance, mood). Management is primarily symptomatic.

## Supportive care infrastructure

Modern chemotherapy requires substantial supportive care infrastructure beyond the drugs themselves.

*Vascular access*. Most chemotherapy is given intravenously, often through central venous access (peripherally inserted central catheters, implanted ports, tunneled catheters). The access devices reduce the morbidity of repeated venous access and allow administration of vesicant drugs (drugs that cause severe tissue damage if extravasated, like anthracyclines and vinca alkaloids). Catheter-related infections and thrombosis are managed with antibiotics and anticoagulation.

*Hydration and electrolyte management*. Especially for cisplatin and methotrexate. Pre- and post-hydration with isotonic fluid, magnesium replacement, sometimes mannitol for cisplatin.

*Infection prevention and treatment*. Neutropenic patients are at substantial infection risk. Prophylactic antibiotics (fluoroquinolones) are sometimes used in high-risk patients. Antifungal prophylaxis (fluconazole, posaconazole) in patients with prolonged neutropenia. Antiviral prophylaxis (acyclovir) for herpes simplex in some settings. Pneumocystis prophylaxis (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) for patients on high-dose corticosteroids or specific regimens.

*Vaccine considerations*. Immunization status should be reviewed. Live vaccines are contraindicated during immunosuppressive treatment. Inactivated vaccines (influenza, COVID-19, pneumococcal) are usually given.

*Nutrition*. Cancer cachexia (involuntary weight loss and muscle wasting) is common and adversely affects outcomes. Nutritional assessment and support, including parenteral nutrition in selected cases, is part of comprehensive care.

*Psychosocial support*. Cancer treatment is psychologically demanding. Depression, anxiety, post-traumatic stress, and family stress all need attention. Cancer centers typically have social workers, psychologists, and support group programs.

*Survivorship care*. After completion of curative-intent treatment, patients enter survivorship phase. Long-term monitoring for cancer recurrence and treatment-related late effects. Cardiovascular monitoring for patients with cardiotoxic treatment. Cancer screening for secondary malignancies. Health behavior counseling (exercise, diet, smoking cessation).

## Combination chemotherapy in practice

The principles of combination chemotherapy described in 22A translate into specific practical considerations:

*Regimen selection*. Choosing between alternative regimens for a given cancer involves consideration of:
- *Efficacy data*. What are the response rates, progression-free survival, overall survival in trials?
- *Toxicity profile*. What are the dose-limiting toxicities? Are they manageable in this patient?
- *Patient factors*. Performance status, comorbidities, prior treatments, patient preferences.
- *Convenience*. Number of cycles, infusion times, oral versus IV, frequency.
- *Cost and access*. Insurance coverage, drug availability.

*Cycle structure*. Most regimens are delivered in cycles of 2-4 weeks. The cycle structure allows time for normal tissue recovery (especially marrow) between treatments. Dose adjustments may be made cycle by cycle based on tolerance and response.

*Treatment duration*. Curative-intent treatment is typically 4-12 cycles (3-9 months) depending on the cancer. Palliative treatment is often continued indefinitely until progression or intolerable toxicity, though "treatment breaks" are increasingly used to provide quality-of-life relief in stable disease.

*Maintenance therapy*. Some regimens include maintenance phases where treatment continues at reduced intensity after initial response. Examples include maintenance pemetrexed in lung cancer, maintenance bevacizumab in some cancers, maintenance rituximab in some lymphomas.

## Special populations

*Elderly patients*. Cancer is largely a disease of older adults, but most chemotherapy trials enroll younger patients. Practical considerations for older patients:
- *Performance status* matters more than chronological age. A fit 80-year-old may tolerate aggressive treatment better than a frail 65-year-old.
- *Comorbidities* (cardiac, renal, hepatic, pulmonary, cognitive) affect treatment selection and tolerance.
- *Polypharmacy* increases drug interaction risk.
- *Geriatric assessment* tools can help identify patients at higher risk of toxicity and may inform treatment intensity.
- Dose reductions or alternative regimens may be appropriate.

*Pediatric oncology*. Children tolerate chemotherapy differently than adults — generally better than expected for many toxicities, but with concerns about long-term effects (growth, fertility, cognitive development, secondary cancers).

*Pregnancy*. Some chemotherapy can be given during pregnancy (typically in second or third trimester) with reasonable outcomes for both mother and baby. The decision is individualized.

*End-stage organ dysfunction*. Chemotherapy in patients with end-stage liver, kidney, or heart disease requires careful drug selection and dose adjustment.

*Comorbid serious illness*. Patients with severe coexisting illness (active infection, severe heart failure, advanced dementia) may not be candidates for chemotherapy. Goals of care discussions are particularly important.

## The cost of chemotherapy

Cancer drugs are expensive. Newer agents can cost $100,000-200,000 or more for a course of treatment. The high costs create access disparities and financial toxicity for patients.

*Financial toxicity*. The economic burden of cancer treatment — out-of-pocket costs, lost income, family financial strain — is increasingly recognized as a form of treatment toxicity. Patients with significant financial toxicity have worse outcomes, partly through non-adherence to treatment.

*Cost-effectiveness analysis*. Drugs are evaluated for cost per quality-adjusted life year (QALY) gained. The thresholds for accepting cost-effectiveness vary by country and setting. Many cancer drugs exceed traditional cost-effectiveness thresholds but are still funded due to disease severity and other factors.

*Generic chemotherapy*. Older chemotherapy drugs are generic and substantially cheaper than newer targeted agents. The classical chemotherapy regimens that produce cures (testicular cancer, lymphoma, ALL) use generic drugs and are highly cost-effective.

*Drug shortages*. Some essential chemotherapy drugs have experienced shortages due to manufacturing issues. The shortages of cisplatin, carboplatin, and methotrexate in 2023 created significant treatment disruptions. The issue reflects the limited profitability of generic drug manufacturing and the consolidation of suppliers.

The economic dimensions of chemotherapy are increasingly recognized as integral to clinical decision-making. Discussions of treatment options should include cost considerations alongside efficacy and toxicity.

## What this chapter gives you

The pharmacology of chemotherapy involves absorption (mostly IV bypass), distribution (with sanctuary sites like the CNS), metabolism (hepatic, with cytochrome P450 enzymes important), and excretion (renal and biliary). Pharmacogenomic variation affects dosing for several drugs (TPMT for thiopurines, UGT1A1 for irinotecan, DPYD for fluoropyrimidines).

Drug resistance mechanisms include reduced uptake, increased efflux (ABC transporters), drug inactivation, target alterations, enhanced DNA repair, apoptosis evasion, cell cycle effects, and microenvironmental protection. Resistance is essentially universal with prolonged chemotherapy use; the rate and mechanism vary by drug and cancer.

The major toxicities span many systems. Myelosuppression (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia) is addressed with growth factors and transfusion. Nausea and vomiting are now substantially controllable with modern antiemetics. Mucositis, fatigue, diarrhea, neuropathy, cardiotoxicity, hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, and pulmonary toxicity each have specific management approaches. Long-term concerns include secondary malignancies, infertility, and cognitive effects.

Supportive care infrastructure includes vascular access, hydration and electrolyte management, infection prevention and treatment, nutritional support, psychosocial support, and survivorship care. The infrastructure has dramatically improved chemotherapy tolerability and outcomes over recent decades.

Combination chemotherapy follows established principles — drugs with different mechanisms, non-overlapping toxicities, demonstrated single-agent activity, and optimal sequencing. Regimen selection considers efficacy, toxicity, patient factors, convenience, and cost.

Special populations (elderly, pediatric, pregnant, comorbid) require individualized approaches. Cost considerations are increasingly important in clinical decision-making.

Chapter 23 turns to hormonal therapies — one of the oldest forms of targeted cancer therapy, used for hormone-driven cancers including breast cancer and prostate cancer. The biology and clinical applications connect to but differ from cytotoxic chemotherapy.
